\title{The Goldbach--Chen Frontier: How the Semiprime Channel Inherits the Singular Series}
\author{Mathias Rodr\'iguez Castro}
\date{Working draft --- \today}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
The binary Goldbach conjecture asks whether every even $N>2$ is a sum of two primes;
Chen's theorem replaces the second prime by a prime or semiprime. We study how the
Goldbach count $R_1(N)$ (prime $q$) and the Chen-surplus count $R_2(N)$ (semiprime $q$)
respond to the Hardy--Littlewood singular series $\sing(N)$. Decomposing the semiprime
channel $q=rs$ by its small prime factor $r$, each channel carries its own two-form
singular series, and in the ratio $R_2/R_1$ the Goldbach singular series \emph{cancels}
(an unconditional Euler-product identity, Lemma~\ref{lem:sing}), leaving a Mertens channel
sum $\widetilde W(N)\sim\log\log N$. Thus $\sing(N)$ enters $R_2$ with exponent $1$,
exactly as in $R_1$: $R_2$ inherits the full singular series. The apparent ``half power''
reported by finite-range log--log fits is therefore not an exponent but a regression
\emph{slope} of a non-power-law object, obeying
$\beta_2(N)=1-c(N)/\langle R_2/R_1\rangle\to1$ with deficit $\sim1/\log\log N$ (a
multi-channel effect: the $3\mid N$ atom accounts for only $\sim10\%$ of it). A
circle-method second-moment bound---using only Vaughan's classical \emph{prime} minor-arc
estimate and the trivial $L^2$ size of the semiprime sum, no sieve---gives the
\emph{linear} almost-all asymptotic $R_2(N)=\mathrm{main}(N)(1+o(1))$ unconditionally in
outline; the passage to the log-regression statement $\beta_2\to1$ is isolated as one
explicitly stated lower-tail hypothesis rather than overclaimed. The same second moment also
controls a \emph{truncated} log-regression statistic, and a blocked-channel model proves the
$1/\log\log N$ deficit. We complement this with
reproducible diagnostics and a gallery of cross-disciplinary reframings, all governed by
the same $\sing(N)$---one fact in many mirrors---of which two (an empirical circle method
whose comet spectrum is the major arcs, and a primorial-base carry geometry) add a
genuinely new computation. The goal is not a proof of Goldbach but a clean new identity,
reproducible evidence, and a bounded reduction.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The binary Goldbach conjecture states that every even integer $N>2$ can be written as the sum of two primes. The conjecture remains open. However, several nearby facts are known. Helfgott proved the ternary, or weak, Goldbach conjecture: every odd integer greater than $5$ is the sum of three primes \citep{helf2014ternary}. Oliveira e Silva, Herzog and Pardi verified the binary Goldbach conjecture computationally up to $4\cdot 10^{18}$ \citep{silva2014}. Chen's theorem proves that every sufficiently large even integer can be written as
\[
  N=p+q,
\]
where $p$ is prime and $q$ is either prime or the product of at most two primes \citep{chen1973}; the semiprime channel and the ``$E_2$'' numbers it counts are classical sieve objects \citep{halberstamrichert}, and for the broader additive-bases context we refer to \citet{nathanson}. Recent sieve-theoretic work also studies intermediate statements between Chen's theorem and the binary Goldbach conjecture \citep{li2026}.

This project focuses on the transition between the Goldbach representation
\[
  N = p + \text{prime}
\]
and the Chen representation
\[
  N = p + \text{semiprime}.
\]
The guiding question is not merely whether a representation exists, but how the available representations are distributed, how often the second term is genuinely prime, how often it is semiprime, and whether a representation can be selected in a smooth way as $N$ varies.

\paragraph{Main idea.}
For each even $N$, define the number of representations with $q$ prime and with $q$ semiprime. Then study the empirical object
\[
  N \longmapsto \{(p,q): p+q=N,\ p\in\PP,\ q\in \PP\cup\SSemi\}.
\]
This is a discrete object, so it is not continuous in the ordinary analytic sense. Nevertheless, one can ask whether the induced empirical measures converge weakly, whether normalized counts have stable asymptotics, and whether a mixed-integer model can select one representation per even integer with small variation. This gives a precise mathematical language for the intuition that the representation may ``jump'' between prime and semiprime behavior.

\paragraph{Summary of findings.}
Sections \ref{sec:empirical}--\ref{sec:balance-emp} report the results of implementing this program; all numbers are reproducible from the accompanying code. The main outcomes are:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em,itemsep=2pt]
\item \textbf{The singular series of $R_2$, and the half-power mirage (\S\ref{sec:singexp}).} Counts are computed for all $N\le2\cdot10^{6}$ by FFT and locally in windows up to $10^{9}$ by a block-convolution sieve. Both $R_1$ and $R_2$ carry $\sing(N)$ with exponent~$1$: in the ratio $R_2/R_1$ the singular series \emph{cancels} (Lemma~\ref{lem:sing}), leaving the Mertens channel sum $\widetilde W(N)\sim\log\log N$. The number $\beta_2\approx\tfrac12$ that a finite-range log--log fit reports is therefore not an exponent but a regression slope of a non-power-law object; a derivation and the data agree on $\beta_2(N)=1-c/\langle R_2/R_1\rangle_N$ with $\langle R_2/R_1\rangle\sim\log\log N$, so $\beta_2\to1$ with an $\mathcal O(1/\log\log N)$ deficit (validation $(1-\beta_2)\langle R_2/R_1\rangle\approx1.09$ to $1\%$). The slope is multi-channel: deleting the heaviest channel ($3\mid N$) removes only $\sim10\%$ of it (\S\ref{sec:singexp}), refuting a single-atom reading.
\item \textbf{Layers and sign reversal (\S\ref{sec:layers-emp}).} For $\OmegaF(q)=k$, the slope $\beta_k$ falls through $1,\tfrac12,\dots$ and becomes negative for $k\ge3$: $\sing(N)$ enhances low-complexity and suppresses high-complexity channels.
\item \textbf{Diagnostics, continuity, optimization (\S\ref{sec:diag-emp}--\ref{sec:balance-emp}).} Factor geometry favours unbalanced semiprimes; the $q/N$ measures stabilize weakly to a near-uniform law; minimum-variation selection is a Pareto trade-off between geometric and type smoothness; the Chen-rescue threshold under a balance constraint is unimodal; and the residual balance exponent $a_{\rm Res}(N)$ correlates positively with $\sing(N)$.
\item \textbf{An almost-all theorem, honestly bounded (Appendix~\ref{app:rigorous}).} The cancellation identity (Lemma~\ref{lem:sing}) is unconditional; a circle-method second-moment bound---Vaughan's classical \emph{prime} minor-arc estimate plus the trivial $L^2$ size of the semiprime sum, no sieve---gives the \emph{linear} asymptotic $R_2(N)=\mathrm{main}(N)(1+o(1))$ for all but $o(X)$ even $N$, unconditional in outline. Upgrading this to the log-regression statement $\beta_2(N)\to1$ needs one further input (lower-tail control of $R_2/R_1$, beyond $L^2$); we state that gap rather than claim past it.
\item \textbf{Many mirrors, one fact (\S\ref{sec:spectral-emp}--\ref{sec:bases-emp}; Appendix~\ref{app:reframings}).} The frontier can be recast as a thermodynamic ground state, an adversarial network, a liquidity market, a control problem, an empirical circle method, an information channel, and a discrete surface. We are explicit that this is \emph{one} finding---$\sing(N)$ sets the local densities---observed many times, not many findings; most reframings are deterministic functions of the same $\sing/R_2$ data and are collected in Appendix~\ref{app:reframings} (with the honest negatives: an empty additive-complexity topology and a Chen substitute with no resilience premium). Two earn a place in the body by yielding a \emph{new computation}: the empirical circle method (\S\ref{sec:spectral-emp}), whose comet power spectrum is literally the major arcs, and the carry geometry (\S\ref{sec:bases-emp}), which surfaces a digit/Kummer signature of primality.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Basic notation}

Let $\PP$ denote the set of primes. For an integer $n\ge2$, let $\OmegaF(n)$ be the number of prime factors of $n$ counted with multiplicity. Thus $\OmegaF(12)=3$ because $12=2^2\cdot 3$.

\begin{definition}[Semiprimes and Chen numbers]
Define
\[
  \SSemi := \{n\ge2 : \OmegaF(n)=2\}
\]
to be the set of semiprimes, allowing squares of primes. Define also
\[
  \Ptwo := \{n\ge2 : \OmegaF(n)\le 2\} = \PP \cup \SSemi.
\]
Thus $\Ptwo$ is the set of integers with at most two prime factors counted with multiplicity.
\end{definition}

For an even integer $N$, define the prime--prime representation count
\begin{equation}
  R_{1}(N) := \sum_{p<N} \ones_{\PP}(p)\ones_{\PP}(N-p).
  \label{eq:R1}
\end{equation}
This counts ordered representations by choosing the first summand $p$. If unordered counts are desired, one may restrict to $p\le N/2$.

Define the prime--semiprime representation count
\begin{equation}
  R_{2}(N) := \sum_{p<N} \ones_{\PP}(p)\ones_{\SSemi}(N-p).
  \label{eq:R2}
\end{equation}
Finally, define the Chen count
\begin{equation}
  R_{\le2}(N) := R_1(N)+R_2(N)
  = \sum_{p<N}\ones_{\PP}(p)\ones_{\Ptwo}(N-p).
  \label{eq:Rle2}
\end{equation}

\begin{remark}
The binary Goldbach conjecture is equivalent to $R_1(N)>0$ for every even $N>2$. Chen's theorem implies $R_{\le2}(N)>0$ for all sufficiently large even $N$.
\end{remark}

\section{The Goldbach--Chen frontier}

The central empirical object is the decomposition of Chen representations into genuinely Goldbach and genuinely semiprime representations:
\[
  R_{\le2}(N) = R_1(N)+R_2(N).
\]
This suggests several diagnostics.

\subsection{Chen surplus and Goldbach fragility}

\begin{definition}[Chen surplus]
For even $N$, define the Chen surplus ratio
\begin{equation}
  C(N) := \frac{R_2(N)}{R_1(N)+1}.
  \label{eq:chen-surplus}
\end{equation}
Large values of $C(N)$ identify integers for which semiprime representations are much more abundant than prime--prime representations.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Goldbach fragility]
For a threshold $k\in\NN$, define the set of $k$-fragile even integers up to $X$ by
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal F_k(X) := \{N\le X: N\equiv0\pmod 2,\ R_1(N)\le k,\ R_2(N)>0\}.
\end{equation}
These are integers for which Goldbach representations exist only sparsely, while Chen-type representations remain available.
\end{definition}

\paragraph{Research question.}
How large is $\mathcal F_k(X)$? Are fragile integers concentrated in specific congruence classes? Does fragility correlate with the singular series correction in the Hardy--Littlewood heuristic?

\subsection{Prime versus semiprime share}

Define
\begin{equation}
  \theta(N) := \frac{R_1(N)}{R_1(N)+R_2(N)},
  \label{eq:theta}
\end{equation}
with the convention $\theta(N)=0$ if the denominator is zero.

Here $\theta(N)$ is the empirical share of Chen representations in which $q$ is prime rather than semiprime. A small value of $\theta(N)$ means that most Chen representations use a genuinely semiprime second summand.

A naive density heuristic suggests
\[
  R_1(N) \asymp \frac{N}{\log^2 N},
  \qquad
  R_2(N) \asymp \frac{N\log\log N}{\log^2 N},
\]
because primes have local density roughly $1/\log N$, while semiprimes have density roughly $\log\log N/\log N$. Therefore, at a first heuristic level, semiprime representations should outnumber prime--prime representations by a factor of order $\log\log N$. This does not prove Goldbach or Chen, but it motivates the following empirical conjecture.

\begin{conjecture}[Average semiprime dominance]
There exists a slowly varying normalization $L(X)\sim \log\log X$ such that
\[
  \frac{1}{X/2}\sum_{\substack{N\le X\\N\equiv0\, (2)}}
  \frac{R_2(N)}{R_1(N)+1}
  \asymp L(X).
\]
Equivalently, prime--semiprime representations should become increasingly abundant relative to prime--prime representations on average.
\end{conjecture}

\section{Distribution of the second summand $q$}

For each even $N$, define the representation sets
\begin{align}
  \mathcal R_1(N) &:= \{(p,q): p+q=N,\ p\in\PP,\ q\in\PP\},\\
  \mathcal R_2(N) &:= \{(p,q): p+q=N,\ p\in\PP,\ q\in\SSemi\},\\
  \mathcal R_{\le2}(N) &:= \mathcal R_1(N)\cup\mathcal R_2(N).
\end{align}

The object $q=N-p$ can be studied in several ways.

\subsection{Location of $q$ inside $[0,N]$}

Define empirical measures on $[0,1]$:
\begin{align}
  \mu_{N}^{(1)} &:= \frac{1}{R_1(N)}\sum_{(p,q)\in\mathcal R_1(N)} \delta_{q/N},\\
  \mu_{N}^{(2)} &:= \frac{1}{R_2(N)}\sum_{(p,q)\in\mathcal R_2(N)} \delta_{q/N},\\
  \mu_{N}^{(\le2)} &:= \frac{1}{R_{\le2}(N)}\sum_{(p,q)\in\mathcal R_{\le2}(N)} \delta_{q/N},
\end{align}
whenever the denominators are nonzero.

\begin{question}[Weak continuity]
Do the measures $\mu_N^{(1)}$, $\mu_N^{(2)}$, or $\mu_N^{(\le2)}$ converge weakly along typical even $N$? If so, is the limit close to the uniform distribution on $(0,1)$ after removing small endpoint effects?
\end{question}

This is a precise version of the intuition that the representation function might become ``continuous'' after smoothing. The pointwise object is discontinuous, because primality is discrete; but the normalized cloud of representations may stabilize in distribution.

\subsection{Type-labeled empirical measures}

Let the type of $q$ be
\[
  \tau(q) :=
  \begin{cases}
    1, & q\in\PP,\\
    2, & q\in\SSemi.
  \end{cases}
\]
Define a type-labeled empirical measure on $[0,1]\times\{1,2\}$ by
\begin{equation}
  \nu_N := \frac{1}{R_{\le2}(N)}
  \sum_{(p,q)\in\mathcal R_{\le2}(N)} \delta_{(q/N,\tau(q))}.
  \label{eq:nuN}
\end{equation}

\begin{conjecture}[Weak type stabilization]
For typical large even $N$, the measures $\nu_N$ are close to a product-like law
\[
  \nu_N \approx f_N(x,t)\,dx\,d\tau,
\]
where the spatial component in $x=q/N$ varies slowly, while the type marginal increasingly favors $t=2$ because semiprimes have larger density than primes.
\end{conjecture}

This is not ordinary continuity. It is closer to convergence of empirical distributions, or continuity after smoothing over windows $N\in[X,X+H]$.

\subsection{Factor geometry of semiprime $q$}

If $q\in\SSemi$, write
\[
  q=ab,
  \qquad a,b\in\PP,
  \qquad a\le b.
\]
Then define the balance statistic
\begin{equation}
  B(q) := \frac{\log a}{\log q}\in \left(0,\frac12\right].
\end{equation}
A value near $1/2$ means that $q$ is a balanced semiprime; a value near $0$ means that $q$ has a very small prime factor.

\begin{question}[Semiprime factor geometry]
Among representations $N=p+q$ with $q\in\SSemi$, how is $B(q)$ distributed? Are Chen representations dominated by unbalanced semiprimes with a small factor, or by balanced semiprimes?
\end{question}

This question is interesting because Chen's theorem is obtained by sieve methods. Empirically, one can ask whether the extra freedom in $q$ comes mostly from allowing small prime divisors, or whether semiprime representations are broadly distributed across factor shapes.

\section{Heuristic asymptotics (classical recovery)}

The asymptotics in this section are classical; we recall them only to fix
normalisation and as a control for the computations of \S\ref{sec:empirical}, not as
new derivations. The Hardy--Littlewood circle-method heuristic for Goldbach
\citep{hardylittlewood1923} predicts a main term of the form
\begin{equation}
  R_1(N) \approx 2C_2\,\sing(N)\frac{N}{\log^2 N},
  \label{eq:HL}
\end{equation}
where $C_2$ is the twin-prime constant and $\sing(N)=\prod_{p\mid N,\,p>2}\frac{p-1}{p-2}$ is
the singular-series correction depending on the odd prime divisors of $N$ (the factor $2C_2$
already absorbs the $p=2$ local density).

For semiprime $q$, Landau's density of numbers with two prime factors
\citep{landau1909}, $\#\{n\le x:\OmegaF(n)=2\}\sim x\log\log x/\log x$, gives the rough
estimate
\begin{equation}
  \#\{n\le x:\OmegaF(n)=2\}
  \sim \frac{x\log\log x}{\log x}.
\end{equation}
Therefore, a first-order independence heuristic gives
\begin{equation}
  R_2(N) \approx K_2(N)\frac{N\log\log N}{\log^2 N},
  \label{eq:R2heuristic}
\end{equation}
where $K_2(N)$ is a correction factor capturing congruence constraints and local arithmetic structure.

\paragraph{Normalization.}
The empirical project should therefore compare
\begin{align}
  A_1(N) &:= \frac{R_1(N)}{N/\log^2 N},\\
  A_2(N) &:= \frac{R_2(N)}{N\log\log N/\log^2 N}.
\end{align}
If the heuristic is meaningful, $A_1(N)$ and $A_2(N)$ should be much more stable than the raw counts.

\section{Selection formulations}
\label{sec:finitemip}

\emph{Scope note.} This section records the optimization \emph{language} used by two
diagnostics measured in \S\ref{sec:opt-emp} (a minimum-variation selection and a
Chen-rescue threshold). It is deliberately short: the feasibility version is trivial and
no MIP solver will prove Goldbach (Remark~\ref{rem:notproof}). The infinite-dimensional and
column-generation readings are recorded in Appendix~\ref{app:infinite}; a reader after the
empirical results may skip to \S\ref{sec:empirical}.

Fix an even $N\le X$ and let $\mathcal A_N^{(1)}=\{p\in\PP:N-p\in\PP\}$ and
$\mathcal A_N^{(2)}=\{p\in\PP:N-p\in\SSemi\}$. Binary variables $z_{N,p,t}$ select one
representation $N=p+q$ of type $t$ (prime $t=1$, semiprime $t=2$); the cover constraint
$\sum_{p\in\mathcal A_N^{(1)}}z_{N,p,1}+\sum_{p\in\mathcal A_N^{(2)}}z_{N,p,2}\ge1$ for every
even $N$ in a window $\EE_X$ is Chen feasibility, and dropping the type-$2$ terms is
Goldbach feasibility. With the sets $\mathcal A_N^{(t)}$ precomputed by a sieve this is
trivial as a feasibility problem; it becomes informative only under added structure.

\begin{remark}[Why this is not a proof strategy]\label{rem:notproof}
These mixed-integer programs do not make Goldbach easier by themselves. They translate the
conjecture into a covering problem over a hypergraph whose vertices are even integers and
whose hyperedges are prime-pair certificates; the value is conceptual, letting one import
selection, smoothness, total variation, and column generation into the empirical study of
Goldbach-type representations (Appendix~\ref{app:infinite}).
\end{remark}

\paragraph{Minimum-variation selection.}
Selecting one representation per even $N$ in a window, with type
$u_N=\sum_{p\in\mathcal A_N^{(2)}}z_{N,p,2}\in\{0,1\}$ and normalized location
$s_N=q_N/N=\sum_{p,t}\frac{N-p}{N}z_{N,p,t}$ (linear in the selection variables), the
minimum-variation MIP minimizes
$\alpha\sum_N v_N+\beta\sum_N r_N+\gamma\sum_N|s_N-\tfrac12|$ subject to one selection per
$N$ and $v_N\ge|u_N-u_{N-2}|$, $r_N\ge|s_N-s_{N-2}|$. The term $\sum v_N$ measures type
oscillation, $\sum r_N$ geometric oscillation, and $|s_N-\tfrac12|$ rewards balance. The
guiding question is whether the minimum type-switch rate $|\EE_X|^{-1}\min\sum_N v_N$
decreases or stays bounded away from $0$ as $X\to\infty$; \S\ref{sec:opt-emp} reports the
answer (a smooth-location branch that nonetheless keeps switching type).

\paragraph{Chen-rescue threshold.}
Restricting representations to a balanced band $p\in[N/2-k,\,N/2]$ and counting how many $N$
\emph{require} a semiprime (no prime pair in the band) defines the rescue fraction, whose
unimodal profile in $k$ is reported in \S\ref{sec:opt-emp}; if the minimum number of
semiprime selections is zero over a finite range, Goldbach holds there.

\section{Empirical results and a new regularity}
\label{sec:empirical}

This section reports the outcome of implementing the program above. All numbers are
reproducible from the accompanying code (see the Data and code availability statement;
the drivers \texttt{run\_counts.py}, \texttt{run\_scaling.py}, \texttt{run\_betalaw.py},
\texttt{run\_estimator\_audit.py}); unless stated otherwise they use the range
$X=2\cdot10^{6}$.

\subsection{Computational setup and validation}

The counts $R_1$ and $R_2$ are obtained for \emph{all} $N\le X$ at once as additive
convolutions,
\[
  R_1=\mathbf 1_{\PP}*\mathbf 1_{\PP},\qquad
  R_2=\mathbf 1_{\PP}*\mathbf 1_{\SSemi},
\]
evaluated by FFT in $O(X\log X)$. For $X=2\cdot10^{6}$ this takes about $15$
seconds including sieves, diagnostics and figures. The FFT counts agree exactly
with a brute-force enumeration on random samples (the rounding error of the
transform is far below $1/2$), and Goldbach's condition $R_1(N)>0$ is verified
throughout the range.

\subsection{The semiprime channel and the half-power mirage}
\label{sec:singexp}

The Hardy--Littlewood prediction \eqref{eq:HL} attaches to $R_1$ the full singular
series $\sing(N)=\prod_{p\mid N,\,p>2}(p-1)/(p-2)$. Regressing
$\log\!\big(R_1/(N/\log^2N)\big)$ against $\log\sing(N)$ recovers, empirically, a slope
$1.000$, and dividing by $\sing(N)$ collapses the coefficient of variation of the
normalized count from $0.39$ to $0.015$ (a $25\times$ reduction); this is a control
that reproduces \eqref{eq:HL} and the band structure of the Goldbach comet.

The new observation concerns $R_2$. We measure how strongly the relative semiprime
share tracks $\sing(N)$ by the \emph{regression slope}
\[
  \beta_2-1:=\text{slope of}\quad
  \log\frac{R_2(N)}{R_1(N)}\quad\text{against}\quad\log\sing(N),
\]
which uses $R_1$ itself as a meter for $\sing(N)$ and is therefore independent of any
chosen normalization. We call $\beta_2$ the \emph{empirical singular exponent}, but
stress at the outset that \textbf{$\beta_2$ is a regression slope, not an exponent of a
power law}: as Lemma~\ref{lem:sing} shows below, $\sing(N)$ enters $R_2$ with exponent
$1$ (exactly as in $R_1$) and \emph{cancels} in the ratio $R_2/R_1$, so $\beta_2$ is the
slope of a non-power-law object and the value $\approx\tfrac12$ reported in finite
ranges is a finite-size correction, not a structural exponent (developed after
Table~\ref{tab:beta}). We adopt one estimator throughout: ordinary least squares of
$\log(R_2/R_1)$ on $\log\sing$, taken \emph{cumulatively} over all even
$5000\le N\le X$. Table~\ref{tab:beta} reports it across several ranges; standard errors
are $\le10^{-4}$ (so the digits shown are significant), and the appendix
(\S\ref{sec:variance}) instead uses a \emph{local} window centred at $X$, which runs
$\approx0.02$--$0.03$ higher at the same $X$---the distinction is between an average over
$[5000,X]$ and a measurement at the leading edge.

\label{fig:exponent}

Over this range, the best finite-range log--log normalization is consistent with the
effective fit (near $X\sim10^{6}$, the exponent $\tfrac12$ drifting to $1$;
\S\ref{sec:singexp} below)
\begin{equation}
  R_2(N)\ \propto\ \sing(N)^{1/2}\,\frac{N}{\log^2 N}\,W(N),
  \qquad
  W(N)=\sum_{\substack{3\le r\le\sqrt N\\ r\ \mathrm{prime}}}\frac{r-1}{r-2}\,\frac1r\,
  \frac{\log N}{\log(N/r)},
  \label{eq:R2law}
\end{equation}
where $W(N)\sim\log\log N$ by Mertens' theorem \citep{hardywright}. The weight $W$ makes precise the
$\log\log N$ factor of \eqref{eq:R2heuristic}: it is a Mertens sum over the
\emph{small prime factor} $r$ of the semiprime $q=rs$, weighted by the
twin-prime-type local factor $(r-1)/(r-2)$. Table~\ref{tab:cv} compares the
dispersion of $R_2$ under three normalizations.

\paragraph{Interpretation: a quantitative Chen rescue effect.}
Because the finite-range slope has $\beta_2<1$, over this range the relative semiprime
share falls with the singular series, $R_2/R_1\propto\sing(N)^{-(1-\beta_2)}$ (locally
$\approx\sing(N)^{-1/2}$): where Goldbach is locally rich (large $\sing$), the prime
channel dominates, whereas where Goldbach is \emph{fragile} (small $\sing$) the semiprime
channel is proportionally most abundant. This makes the Chen-rescue intuition---the
semiprime channel matters most exactly where primes are scarce---quantitative as an
\emph{effective finite-window scaling}, with the deficit $1-\beta_2\asymp1/\log\log N$
vanishing asymptotically (so it is a slope, not a fixed power; \S\ref{sec:singexp}
opening). Heuristically the slope sits below $1$ because writing $q=rs$ couples the
divisors of $N$ to the factor $r$:
when a small prime $\ell\mid N$, the channel $r=\ell$ is \emph{blocked}
(then $p=N-\ell s\equiv0\bmod\ell$ forces $p=\ell$), removing its term from the sum
over channels precisely when $\sing(N)$ is large (Lemma~\ref{lem:sing}). This
explains the direction $\beta_2<1$; the precise value is not $1/2$, as the scaling
analysis below shows.

\paragraph{Scaling to $10^9$: the slope grows like $\log\log X$.}
Measuring $\beta_2$ \emph{locally} in a window of $2\cdot10^{6}$ even integers
centred at $X$ (computed by a block-convolution sieve, so that $X$ can reach
$10^{9}$ while only the window is held in memory), the slope does \emph{not}
settle at $1/2$: it rises monotonically (Fig.~\ref{fig:scaling}). Eight windows
from $6\cdot10^{6}$ to $10^{9}$ are fit to within $0.0013$ by
\begin{equation}
  \beta_2(X)\ \approx\ -0.094(7)+0.234(3)\,\log\log X
  \qquad (R^2=0.9987,\ \text{8 windows}),
  \label{eq:beta-loglog}
\end{equation}
the parenthesised figures being the last-digit standard errors of the two-parameter
fit. Thus the half-power $\beta_2\approx\tfrac12$ is only the \emph{local} value near
$X\sim10^{6}$, and the slope is not a constant. The fit reaches $\beta_2\approx0.61$ at
$10^{9}$ (measured). We deliberately \emph{do not} read a numerical limit off
\eqref{eq:beta-loglog}: extrapolating a $\log\log X$ trend fitted over three decades to,
say, $X\sim10^{46}$ (where it would touch $\beta_2=1$) is well outside the data and is
not evidence---the robust content is the \emph{functional form} $1-\beta_2\asymp
1/\log\log X$ and its derivation below, not a forecast of where the slope crosses a
particular value. The regression quality stays high throughout ($R^2\approx0.997$,
$\mathrm{CV}$ of the half-power normalization $\lesssim0.04$).

\paragraph{A derivation: $\beta_2\to1$ with an $\mathbf{1/\log\log N}$ deficit.}
The drift is not arbitrary. Writing $q=N-p=rs$ and summing over the small factor
$r$, each channel is a Goldbach count for the linear form $(s,N-rs)$, whose
two-form singular series factorizes as
\begin{equation}
  \sing_2(r,N)=
  \begin{cases}2C_2\,\sing(N)\,\dfrac{r-1}{r-2},&r\nmid N,\\[1mm]0,&r\mid N\end{cases}
  \label{eq:S2factor}
\end{equation}
(Lemma~\ref{lem:sing}; the $r\mid N$ channel is identically blocked). Since
$R_1=2C_2\sing(N)I_1$ and $R_2=2C_2\sing(N)\sum_{r\nmid N}\frac{r-1}{r-2}I_r$, the
ratio \emph{cancels the singular series exactly},
\begin{equation}
  \frac{R_2(N)}{R_1(N)}=\widetilde W(N):=\sum_{\substack{3\le r\le\sqrt N\\r\nmid N}}\frac{r-1}{r-2}\,w_r(N),
  \qquad w_r=\frac1r\frac{\log N}{\log(N/r)} .
  \label{eq:Wtilde}
\end{equation}
Hence the measured exponent is a regression slope,
$\beta_2-1=\operatorname{Cov}(\log\widetilde W,\log\sing)/\operatorname{Var}(\log\sing)$,
and the only coupling to $\sing(N)$ comes from the blocked channels $r\mid N$, each
\emph{removed} from $\widetilde W$. Modelling $\mathbf 1[\ell\mid N]$ as independent
with probability $1/\ell$, $\operatorname{Var}(\log\sing)=:B$ is a convergent constant,
while the removed terms contribute
$\operatorname{Cov}(\log\widetilde W,\log\sing)=-A/\widetilde W_0$ with $A>0$
constant and $\widetilde W_0\approx\langle R_2/R_1\rangle\sim\log\log N$ the baseline
sum (Theorem~\ref{thm:beta}). Therefore
\begin{equation}
  \beta_2(N)=1-\frac{c(N)}{\langle R_2/R_1\rangle_N}+o\!\left(\frac1{\log\log N}\right),
  \qquad c(N)=\tfrac{A}{B}=O(1)\ (\approx1.1\text{ in range; Fig.~\ref{fig:constant}}),
  \label{eq:betalaw}
\end{equation}
so $\beta_2\to1$: \emph{$R_2$ asymptotically inherits the full Goldbach singular
series}, the approach being governed by $1/\log\log N$. This explains why finite
ranges show $\beta_2\approx\tfrac12$ and a slow apparent drift.

\begin{proposition}[Validation, non-circular]
The law \eqref{eq:betalaw} predicts that $(1-\beta_2)\langle R_2/R_1\rangle$ is
constant. Measured over windows from $6\cdot10^{6}$ to $2\cdot10^{8}$ it equals
$1.11,1.11,1.10,1.09,1.09,1.08$, a coefficient of variation of $1\%$ (versus
$1.7\%$ for $(1-\beta_2)\log\log N$): the correct ``clock'' is
$\langle R_2/R_1\rangle$, exactly as \eqref{eq:betalaw} requires
(Fig.~\ref{fig:betalaw}).
\end{proposition}

\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{12_betalaw.png}
\caption{The derived law: $\beta_2$ against $1/\langle R_2/R_1\rangle\sim1/\log\log N$
is a straight line through the limit point $(0,1)$, i.e.\ $\beta_2\to1$.}

\end{figure}

\paragraph{Not a single-atom effect.}
A natural worry is that the whole slope is the arithmetic of one prime: $3\mid N$
simultaneously doubles $\sing(N)$ (the factor $\frac{3-1}{3-2}=2$) and deletes the
heaviest semiprime channel $r=3$, so one might suspect $\beta_2\approx\tfrac12$ is just
``what divisibility by $3$ does'' near $X\sim10^{6}$. The data refute this. Recomputing
the slope on the subpopulation $3\nmid N$ (the $r=3$ atom removed) gives, at
$X=2\cdot10^{6}$, $\beta_2=0.547\pm0.0004$ against $0.495$ on all $N$: the deficit
$1-\beta_2$ falls by only about $10\%$ (\texttt{run\_estimator\_audit.py}). The mechanism
is real---conditional means confirm $\langle\log\sing\mid 3\mid N\rangle-\langle\log\sing\mid 3\nmid N\rangle=0.69\approx\log2$ and a depressed
$R_2/R_1$ on $3\mid N$---but it accounts for a tenth of the slope, not the bulk. The
negativity of $\beta_2-1$ is genuinely a \emph{sum over channels} $r=3,5,7,\dots$, each
blocked on the sparse set $r\mid N$, exactly as $A(X)=\sum_r(\cdots)$ in
\eqref{eq:betalaw}; it is not the signature of a single residue class.

\paragraph{Sign reversal of the singular response.}
The Goldbach singular series \emph{enhances} the low-complexity channels
($\beta_1=1$, $\beta_2\approx\tfrac12>0$) but \emph{suppresses} the high-complexity
ones ($\beta_k<0$ for $k\ge3$): where $\sing(N)$ is large, representations with a
many-factored $q$ become rarer. The mechanism is the same divisor effect seen for
$R_2$, now run in reverse. When $\sing(N)$ is large, $N$ is divisible by many small
primes; the admissible $p$ avoid those residues, which forces $q=N-p$ to also avoid
small prime factors, so $q$ tends to have \emph{fewer} prime factors. Thus a large
singular series biases $q$ toward low arithmetic complexity. All $\beta_k$ drift
upward with $X$ at the same slow rate as $\beta_2$ (e.g.\ $\beta_3$ moves from
$-0.30$ at $2\cdot10^{6}$ to $-0.20$ at $8\cdot10^{6}$), so the crossing point of
$\beta_k=0$ moves slowly to the right. We also find that the strongest
``rescue'' channel, $q=$ composite$\times$composite (both factors composite), is
available for \emph{every} even $N$ in the range with on average $\sim1.8\cdot10^{4}$
representations: the additive hierarchy is extremely redundant below the prime
layer. The whole sign reversal is the coefficient-level shadow of a single
generating-function response in the blocked-channel model, made precise in
Appendix~\ref{sec:omega-gf}.

\subsection{Optimization experiments}
\label{sec:opt-emp}

\paragraph{Smoothness is a Pareto trade-off.}
Minimizing the total spatial variation $\sum|s_N-s_{N-2}|$ of the selected location
$s_N=q_N/N$ yields a geometrically very smooth branch (total variation $0.0040$ over
$201$ consecutive even integers) that nonetheless \emph{switches type in $28\%$ of
its steps} and uses semiprimes $72\%$ of the time (Fig.~\ref{fig:branch}). Forbidding
semiprimes (the pure Goldbach branch, with zero type switches) raises the variation
to $0.0246$, a factor of $6.2$ rougher. One cannot simultaneously maximize geometric
and type smoothness: continuity in location and chaos in type coexist.

\paragraph{A unimodal Chen-rescue threshold.}
Restricting representations to a balanced band $p\in[N/2-k,\,N/2]$ and measuring the
fraction of $N$ that \emph{require} a semiprime (no prime pair in the band) gives a
unimodal curve in $k$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:rescue}). For $k\gtrsim0.005\,N$ the prime
channel essentially never fails; the rescue fraction peaks near $38$--$40\%$ at an
intermediate half-width $k\sim20$--$40$; and for $k=1,2$ it falls again because
often no representation exists at all. The peak shifts right with $N$ (roughly as
$\log^2N$), marking where the expected number of balanced prime pairs,
$\sim2C_2\,\sing(N)\,k/\log^2(N/2)$, drops to $O(1)$ -- a Poisson-type transition.
This is the regime captured by the residual exponent $a_{\rm Res}(N)$ of
\S\ref{sec:balance-emp}: Chen's semiprime flexibility becomes \emph{necessary} precisely
when near-perfect balance is imposed.

\subsection{Weak continuity of the location measures}
\label{sec:cont-emp}

Finally we test the weak-continuity intuition of \S4 (Question~1 and the weak
type-stabilization hypothesis there) directly. The pointwise measure $\mu_N$ of $q/N$ is noisy, but
averaging over a window $N\in[X,X+H]$ produces a stable density
(Fig.~\ref{fig:continuity}, left). Three findings, at $X=10^{6}$:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
\item \textbf{The smoothed measure converges.} Splitting a window into two
same-scale subsamples, the total-variation distance between their densities falls
monotonically from $2.1\cdot10^{-3}$ to $3\cdot10^{-4}$ as $H$ grows from $125$ to
$4000$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:continuity}, right), i.e.\ the per-$N$ noise averages out --
a concrete form of weak convergence.
\item \textbf{The limit is essentially uniform.} The prime-$q$ density is within
total variation $0.028$ of the uniform law on $(0,1)$, with only a mild upturn near
the endpoints (the $1/(\log(xN)\log((1-x)N))$ effect). This answers Question~1
affirmatively: after removing endpoint effects, $\mu_N^{(1)}$ is close to uniform.
\item \textbf{Prime and semiprime locations nearly coincide.} The two densities are
within total variation $0.019$ of each other (Fig.~\ref{fig:continuity}, center),
confirming the weak type-stabilization picture. The semiprime location is slightly skewed toward larger
$q$ (mean $q/N=0.513$ versus the exactly symmetric $0.500$ for primes), the only
visible signature that $p$ and $q$ play asymmetric roles in $R_2$.
\end{itemize}

Appendix~\ref{sec:weak-cont} gives the weighted Hardy--Littlewood explanation: after
normalization, both the inherited singular series and the Mertens channel sum cancel,
leaving the uniform law for $q/N$ in either channel (conditional on the weighted
channel asymptotics).

\subsection{The residual Goldbach--Chen balance exponent}
\label{sec:balance-emp}

Inspired by the $(1+a)$ interpolation of Li and Liu (every large even $N$ is
$p+rq$ with $r\le q^{a-1}$, so that $(1+1)$ is Goldbach and $(1+2)$ is Chen), one
can ask not whether a \emph{fixed} exponent works, but the smallest exponent
\emph{needed} for each $N$. For the nondegenerate Chen layer $N=p+rs$ ($p,r,s$
prime, $r\le s$), define the residual balance exponent and minimal multiplier
\[
  a_{\rm Res}(N)=1+\min_{N=p+rs}\frac{\log r}{\log s},
  \qquad
  r_\star(N)=\min\{r\in\PP:\exists\,p,s\in\PP,\ N=p+rs\}.
\]
We compute both exactly for all even $N\le2\cdot10^{6}$ ($r_\star$ by FFT channel
coverage; $a_{\rm Res}$ by a small-$p$ scan, validated against full enumeration to
$0$ error). Findings (Fig.~\ref{fig:balexp}, $N\ge1000$):

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
\item \textbf{Typical residual collapse.} The median $a_{\rm Res}$ \emph{decreases}
with $X$ ($1.115\to1.099\to1.088$ at $X=10^5,5\cdot10^5,2\cdot10^6$), with
$p_{99}=1.182$ and a finite-range maximum of $1.385$ -- far below the Li--Liu
threshold $1.9$. The worst case is pinned by a few small ``hard'' $N$.
\item \textbf{The minimal multiplier is tiny.} $r_\star=3$ for $68\%$ of $N$ and the
dictionary $\{2,3,5,7\}$ already covers $99.7\%$; $\{2,\dots,11\}$ covers
$99.99\%$. This settles the multiplier-dictionary question empirically.
Appendix~\ref{sec:least-unblocked} explains the dictionary through a
least-unblocked-prime law: conditionally on fixed-channel almost-all asymptotics,
$r_\star(N)$ equals the least odd prime not dividing $N$ for almost all even $N$ (so
$\Pr(r_\star\in\{3,5,7\})\approx0.99$ in the divisibility model).
\item \textbf{Local obstruction.} $r_\star$ jumps when a small prime divides $N$: if
$3\mid N$ the channel $r=3$ is blocked (then $p=N-3s\equiv0\bmod3$), and indeed the
mean $r_\star$ is $4.59$ for $3\mid N$ versus $2.94$ otherwise.
\item \textbf{Link to the singular series.} $a_{\rm Res}$ correlates
\emph{positively} with $\log\sing(N)$ (correlation $+0.59$). The very divisibility
of $N$ by small primes that enlarges $\sing(N)$ (boosting Goldbach) also blocks the
small-$r$ Chen channels, so the residual balance is \emph{worst exactly where
Goldbach is locally richest}. This ties the balance exponent to the singular-series
theme of \S\ref{sec:layers-emp}.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Three further probes: transport, topology, dynamics}
\label{sec:further-emp}

We close with three geometric and statistical probes of the frontier; each, perhaps
surprisingly, returns to the singular series.

\paragraph{Optimal transport (reflection defect).}
Under the reflection $T(x)=1-x$ (which sends $p/N$ to $q/N$), compare the prime
location law $\alpha$ (density of $p/N$) with the reflected layer laws
$T_\#\beta_k$. The reflection defects in $W_1$ are
$\Delta_1=W_1(\alpha,T_\#\beta_1)=0.040$ and $\Delta_2=W_1(\alpha,T_\#\beta_2)=0.032$,
and the optimal Chen mixture attains $\Delta_{\le2}=0.032$ at $\lambda^\star=0$: the
reflected \emph{semiprime} cloud matches the primes \emph{better} than the reflected
primes do, a $22\%$ ``transport collapse'' (stable in $X$), driven by unbalanced
semiprimes with a small factor (Fig.~\ref{fig:transport}).

Appendix~\ref{app:reframings}, since they mostly re-express singular-series governance
rather than constitute independent findings (we record there the honest negatives too: the
topology probe is empty and the Chen substitute carries no resilience premium).

\subsection{An empirical circle method: the spectrum of the comet}
\label{sec:spectral-emp}

The circle method decomposes additive problems by Fourier analysis into major arcs
(rationals $a/q$ with small $q$, carrying the singular series) and minor arcs (the
pseudorandom remainder). One can see this decomposition \emph{empirically}. Detrending
$R_1$ over a window of consecutive even integers and taking its power spectrum
(Fig.~\ref{fig:spectral}, left) reveals a forest of sharp lines exactly at the arithmetic
frequencies: the dominant peak is at $\tfrac13$ (period $T_N=6$, the modulus $3$), followed
by $\tfrac15,\tfrac25$ (modulus $5$), with heights that decay across primes in step with the
singular-series factors $(\ell-1)/(\ell-2)$. These are the major arcs made visible.

Dually, one can ask how much of the comet's oscillation is captured by purely local
information. Predicting the detrended $\log R_1(N)$ from the residue $N\bmod\prod_{\ell\le B}\ell$
explains $84.6\%$ of the variance at $B=3$ (i.e.\ $N\bmod6$ alone), $95.0\%$ at $B=5$, and
saturates toward the singular-series ceiling $R^2=0.999$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:spectral}, right).
The comet is, to first order, a \emph{modulo-$6$ phenomenon}: the prime $3$ supplies $85\%$
of all explainable structure, and successive primes contribute the rapidly shrinking
remainder predicted by $\sing(N)$.

\subsection{Primorial bases and carry geometry}
\label{sec:bases-emp}

Two further reframings come from writing integers in a base. In a primorial base
$B_y=\prod_{p\le y}p$, every large prime is a unit modulo $B_y$, so the residues
admissible for $N$ are $A_y(N)=\{a\in(\mathbb Z/B_y)^\times:N-a\in(\mathbb Z/B_y)^\times\}$,
with local coverage $\lambda_y(N)=|A_y(N)|/\phi(B_y)$. By the Chinese remainder theorem this
factorises, and the \emph{relative} coverage is precisely the truncated singular series,
\[
  \frac{\lambda_y(N)}{\lambda_y(\text{generic})}
  =\prod_{\substack{p\mid N\\3\le p\le y}}\frac{p-1}{p-2}=\sing_y(N).
\]
So primorial coverage \emph{is} the singular series, viewed as accumulated local
obstructions; predicting $\log R_1$ from $\sing_y$ explains $84.6\%$ of the comet's
oscillation at $y=3$ ($B_3=6$), rising to $99.8\%$ by $y=30$---the same saturation as
\S\ref{sec:spectral-emp}, now read as ``the comet is the shadow of primorial local
coverage''.

The genuinely new object is the \emph{carry geometry}. Adding $p+q=N$ in base $b$ produces
carries $c_i\in\{0,1\}$, and their number is fixed by the digit sums,
$\#\text{carries}(p,N-p)=\big(S_b(p)+S_b(N-p)-S_b(N)\big)/(b-1)$; in base $2$ this is
$v_2\binom{N}{p}$ (Kummer). Comparing Goldbach representations to random pairs $a+(N-a)=N$
reveals a systematic \emph{carry excess}: Goldbach pairs carry more (Fig.~\ref{fig:bases}).
In base $2$ the excess is $+0.97$ carries (Goldbach $9.9$ versus $8.9$), and a positive
excess persists in every base tested ($b=2,\dots,16$), largest in the small bases. The
origin is arithmetic: a prime's last digit is coprime to $b$, which biases the digit sums
$S_b(p)\equiv p\pmod{b-1}$ away from the values that would avoid carries. Thus the same
local constraints that produce the singular series leave a \emph{digital signature}:
Goldbach decompositions are, bit for bit, slightly carry-heavier than chance---a
statistical-mechanical fingerprint of primality in the geometry of addition.

\end{figure}

\section{Conclusion}

The one durable result of this study is a single Euler-product identity and its
consequence: decomposing the semiprime channel by its small prime factor shows that the
Goldbach singular series $\sing(N)$ enters $R_2$ with exponent $1$, exactly as it enters
$R_1$, and \emph{cancels} in the ratio $R_2/R_1=\widetilde W(N)$ (Lemma~\ref{lem:sing}).
The widely tabulated ``$\beta_2\approx\tfrac12$'' is therefore not an exponent but a
regression slope of a non-power-law object, drifting to $1$ like $1-c/\log\log N$; we
verify this non-circularly, show it is a multi-channel effect (not the $3\mid N$ atom),
and reduce the limit to classical inputs---unconditionally for the \emph{linear}
almost-all asymptotic $R_2=\mathrm{main}(1+o(1))$, and, for the log-regression statement
$\beta_2\to1$, modulo one explicitly stated lower-tail hypothesis
(Corollary~\ref{cor:beta-aa}). A truncated-log proposition and a blocked-channel model
(\S\ref{sec:truncated}) sharpen this gap: the second moment already controls a stable
logarithmic statistic and the model proves the $1/\log\log X$ deficit, while only the
untruncated log-regression still requires lower-tail input.

We have deliberately resisted the temptation to read this one fact as many. The
optimization formulations give a language, not a proof strategy
(Remark~\ref{rem:notproof}); the dozen physical, economic, and information-theoretic
reframings are, with two exceptions (the empirical circle method and the carry geometry),
the same singular-series governance re-photographed in different filters, and we have said
so rather than count them as separate findings. The negatives---an empty additive-complexity
topology, a Chen substitute with no resilience premium---are reported as negatives.

This is not a proof of Goldbach. It is reproducible evidence, one clean new identity, and
a tightly bounded reduction, at the boundary between additive number theory, computational
experimentation, and discrete optimization.

\appendix
\section{Towards a proof that \texorpdfstring{$\beta_2\to1$}{beta2 to 1}}
\label{app:rigorous}

We make the law \eqref{eq:betalaw} precise. We cannot prove it unconditionally:
the asymptotics of $R_1,R_2$ are themselves the Hardy--Littlewood conjecture, and
even $R_1(N)>0$ is open. We therefore isolate what is unconditional (Lemmas
\ref{lem:sing}--\ref{lem:moments}), prove the law \emph{conditionally} on a
quantitative Hardy--Littlewood hypothesis (Theorem~\ref{thm:beta}), and note that it
holds unconditionally in the Cram\'er random model.

\subsection{Notation and the statistic}
Fix a window $\mathcal N_X=\{N\equiv0\,(2):X\le N<X+H\}$ with $H=H(X)\to\infty$,
$H=o(X)$. Write $\mathbb E_X,\Var_X,\operatorname{Cov}_X$ for empirical moments over
$\mathcal N_X$, $g_\ell=\log\frac{\ell-1}{\ell-2}$, and define the local singular
exponent through the (least-squares) regression slope
\begin{equation}
  \beta_2(X)-1:=\frac{\operatorname{Cov}_X\!\big(\log(R_2/R_1),\,\log\sing\big)}
                     {\Var_X\!\big(\log\sing\big)} .
  \label{eq:beta-def}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Unconditional lemmas}

\begin{lemma}[Singular series of a channel]\label{lem:sing}
Let $r$ be an odd prime and $N$ even. The singular series of the pair of linear
forms $s\mapsto(s,\,N-rs)$ is
\[
  \sing_2(r,N)=\prod_{\ell}\frac{1-\nu_r(\ell)/\ell}{(1-1/\ell)^2},
  \qquad \nu_r(\ell)=\#\{s\bmod\ell:\ s(N-rs)\equiv0\},
\]
and equals
\[
  \sing_2(r,N)=
  \begin{cases}
   2C_2\,\sing(N)\,\dfrac{r-1}{r-2}, & r\nmid N,\\[2mm]
   0, & r\mid N,
  \end{cases}
  \qquad C_2=\prod_{p>2}\Big(1-\tfrac1{(p-1)^2}\Big).
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Compute $\nu_r(\ell)$. For $\ell=2$: as $s$ and $N-rs$ are both odd exactly when
$s$ is odd, $\nu_r(2)=1$ and the factor is $(1-\tfrac12)/(1-\tfrac12)^2=2$. For odd
$\ell\nmid rN$: $s\equiv0$ and $s\equiv Nr^{-1}\not\equiv0$ are two distinct roots,
$\nu_r=2$, factor $(1-2/\ell)/(1-1/\ell)^2$. For odd $\ell\mid N$, $\ell\nmid r$:
$N-rs\equiv-rs$ vanishes iff $s\equiv0$, the same root as $s\equiv0$, so $\nu_r=1$,
factor $(1-1/\ell)/(1-1/\ell)^2$. For $\ell=r\nmid N$: $N-rs\equiv N\not\equiv0$, so
only $s\equiv0$, $\nu_r=1$, same factor. Taking
$\prod_{\ell>2}(1-2/\ell)/(1-1/\ell)^2=C_2$ as the base and converting each special
factor relative to it multiplies by $(\ell-1)/(\ell-2)$; the special primes are
exactly those dividing $N$ (giving $\prod_{\ell\mid N}=\sing(N)$ when $r\nmid N$)
together with $\ell=r$ (giving $(r-1)/(r-2)$). This proves the first case. If
$r\mid N$ then $N-rs\equiv0\bmod r$ for \emph{all} $s$, so $\nu_r(r)=r$, the factor
$1-r/r=0$ vanishes, and $\sing_2(r,N)=0$: the channel is identically blocked because
$p=N-rs\equiv0\bmod r$ forces $p=r$.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Moments of $\log\sing$]\label{lem:moments}
For distinct odd primes $\ell_1,\dots,\ell_k$,
$\#\{N\in\mathcal N_X:\ell_i\mid N\ \forall i\}=|\mathcal N_X|\prod_i\ell_i^{-1}+O(2^k)$.
Consequently the indicators $e_\ell=\mathbf 1[\ell\mid N]$ are, as $X\to\infty$,
independent Bernoulli$(1/\ell)$ in all joint moments, and for any weights $a_\ell$ with
$\sum a_\ell^2/\ell<\infty$,
\[
  \mathbb E_X\Big[\textstyle\sum_\ell a_\ell e_\ell\Big]\to\sum_\ell\frac{a_\ell}{\ell},
  \qquad
  \Var_X\Big[\textstyle\sum_\ell a_\ell e_\ell\Big]\to
  \sum_\ell a_\ell^2\,\frac1\ell\Big(1-\frac1\ell\Big).
\]
In particular $\Var_X(\log\sing)\to B:=\sum_{\ell>2}g_\ell^2\,\ell^{-1}(1-\ell^{-1})$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The count is inclusion of the arithmetic progression $0\bmod\prod\ell_i$ in an
interval of $\tfrac12|\mathcal N_X|$ even integers, giving the stated main term and
$O(2^k)$ boundary error. Hence joint factorial moments of the $e_\ell$ converge to
those of independent Bernoulli$(1/\ell)$, and the moment formulas follow by the
dominated convergence afforded by $\sum a_\ell^2/\ell<\infty$ (here
$a_\ell=g_\ell\asymp1/\ell$).
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
Lemma~\ref{lem:moments} is sharp numerically: at $X=10^{8}$, $H=2\cdot10^{6}$ one
finds $\Var_X(\log\sing)=0.12620$ against $B=0.12620$, and
$\mathbb E_X[\log\sing]=0.34842=\sum_\ell g_\ell/\ell$ to five places.
\end{remark}

\subsection{The conditional theorem}

\begin{assumption}[Quantitative Hardy--Littlewood]\label{ass:HL}
There is $\delta>0$ such that, uniformly for odd primes $r\le\sqrt N$ with $r\nmid N$,
\[
  C_r'(N):=\#\{p,s\in\PP:\,p+rs=N,\ s\ge r\}
  =\sing_2(r,N)\,I_r(N)\big(1+O(\log^{-\delta}N)\big),
\]
with $I_r(N)=\int_{2}^{N/r}\frac{dt}{\log t\,\log(N-rt)}$, and the same for
$R_1(N)=2C_2\sing(N)I_1(N)(1+O(\log^{-\delta}N))$, $I_1(N)=\int_2^{N}\frac{dt}{\log t\,\log(N-t)}$.
\end{assumption}

This is the standard prime $k$-tuple heuristic made quantitative and \emph{uniform in
the coefficient $r$}; the uniformity is the genuine analytic difficulty (it is the
arena of the sieve work surrounding Proposition $(1+a)$).

\begin{theorem}[$\beta_2\to1$, conditional on Assumption~\ref{ass:HL}]\label{thm:beta}
Under Assumption~\ref{ass:HL}, for $N\in\mathcal N_X$,
\[
  \frac{R_2(N)}{R_1(N)}=\widetilde W(N)\big(1+o(1)\big),\qquad
  \widetilde W(N)=\sum_{\substack{3\le r\le\sqrt N\\ r\nmid N}}\frac{r-1}{r-2}\,w_r(N),
  \quad w_r=\frac{I_r}{I_1}=\frac1r\frac{\log N}{\log(N/r)}\big(1+o(1)\big),
\]
the Goldbach singular series $\sing(N)$ cancelling by Lemma~\ref{lem:sing}.
Writing $\widetilde W_0(X)=\mathbb E_X[\widetilde W]\sim\log\log X$ and linearising
$\log\widetilde W$ about $\widetilde W_0$,
\[
  \beta_2(X)-1=-\frac{c(X)}{\widetilde W_0(X)}+O\!\big(\widetilde W_0^{-2}\big),
  \qquad
  c(X)=\frac{A(X)}{B},\quad
  A(X)=\sum_{r>2}\frac{r-1}{r-2}\,w_r(X)\,g_r\,\frac1r\Big(1-\frac1r\Big),
\]
both $A(X)$ and $B$ being absolutely convergent (and $A$ formed with the \emph{exact}
channel weights $I_r/I_1$, not their leading approximation). Thus $c(X)$ is an explicit
$O(1)$ constant: the leading-order weights give $c\approx1.0$, the exact $I_r/I_1$ raise
it to the measured $c\approx1.1$, and $c(X)$ decreases slowly with $X$. The precise limit
$c_0=\lim_X c(X)$ is delicate (it depends on the exact weights and averaging convention),
but is immaterial to the conclusion, since the \emph{order} is robust:
\[
  \boxed{\ \beta_2(X)=1-\frac{c(X)}{\langle R_2/R_1\rangle_X}\ \longrightarrow\ 1,\qquad
  c(X)\asymp1,\qquad 1-\beta_2(X)\asymp\frac{1}{\log\log X}.\ }
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
By Lemma~\ref{lem:sing}, $R_2=\sum_{r\nmid N}\sing_2(r,N)I_r=2C_2\sing(N)\sum_{r\nmid N}\frac{r-1}{r-2}I_r$
and $R_1=2C_2\sing(N)I_1$, so under Assumption~\ref{ass:HL} the ratio is
$\widetilde W(N)(1+O(\log^{-\delta}N))$ and $\sing(N)$ cancels. Only the channels
$r\mid N$ are special, each \emph{removed} from $\widetilde W$ (not merely
reweighted); thus $\widetilde W(N)=\widetilde W_0-\sum_{r}\frac{r-1}{r-2}w_r e_r$ up
to the slow variation of $w_r$. Linearising,
$\log\widetilde W=\log\widetilde W_0-\widetilde W_0^{-1}\sum_r\frac{r-1}{r-2}w_r e_r+O(\widetilde W_0^{-2})$.
Insert into \eqref{eq:beta-def}: with $\log\sing=\sum_\ell g_\ell e_\ell$ and the
asymptotic independence of Lemma~\ref{lem:moments}, only the diagonal $r=\ell$ terms
survive, giving $\operatorname{Cov}_X=-\widetilde W_0^{-1}A(X)$ and
$\Var_X=B$, whence the displayed slope. Finally $\widetilde W_0(X)=\mathbb E_X[\widetilde W]
=\sum_{3\le r\le\sqrt X}\frac{r-1}{r-2}\frac1r\frac{\log X}{\log(X/r)}(1-1/r)
=\log\log X+O(1)$ by Mertens, and equals $\langle R_2/R_1\rangle_X$ up to $o(1)$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Confirmation and the unconditional model}
Numerically (Fig.~\ref{fig:betalaw}; \texttt{run\_betalaw.py}): the prediction
$\widetilde W(N)$ regressed against $\log\sing$ reproduces the measured $\beta_2$ with
the correct drift ($0.554,0.583,0.602$ at $X=2\!\cdot\!10^{6},2\!\cdot\!10^{7},10^{8}$
versus measured $0.531,0.567,0.589$), and the explicit constant
$c(X)=A/B$ matches the measured $(1-\beta_2)\langle R_2/R_1\rangle\approx1.1$ in order and
sign; with the leading weights $w_r$ one gets $c\approx1.0$, the residual gap being the
crude approximation $w_r\approx I_r/I_1$ (exact agreement to $0.4\%$ once $I_r$ is used,
Remark in \S\ref{sec:majorarc}) together with the linearisation remainder. In the
Cram\'er model, where
$\mathbf1_\PP,\mathbf1_{\SSemi}$ are replaced by independent variables with the
correct local densities and divisibility, Assumption~\ref{ass:HL} holds by
construction and Theorem~\ref{thm:beta} is unconditional.

\paragraph{The rate constant is not cleanly determined.}
The natural ``constant'' admits two forms, $\kappa(X)=(1-\beta_2)\log\log X$ and
$c(X)=(1-\beta_2)\langle R_2/R_1\rangle$, which agree in order but not in value
because $\langle R_2/R_1\rangle=\log\log X+O(1)$. Both \emph{decrease} over the
computed range (Fig.~\ref{fig:constant}): $\kappa=1.25,1.22,1.20,1.18$ and
$c=1.12,1.10,1.09,1.08$ at $X=2\cdot10^{6},2\cdot10^{7},10^{8},5\cdot10^{8}$.
A linear fit in $1/\log\log X$ extrapolates to $\kappa_\infty\approx0.54$ and
$c_\infty\approx0.73$---with a correction term of slope $\approx1.9$, larger than
the limit itself. Two conclusions follow. First, the limiting constant is
\emph{normalisation-dependent} ($0.54$ versus $0.73$), so no single number is
canonical. Second, the approach is correction-dominated: the finite-range value
$\approx1.1$ is mostly the $1/\log\log X$ term, and the true limit ($\approx0.5$--$0.7$,
itself uncertain) lies far below it and is \emph{not reliably determinable} from data
up to $10^{9}$. (Our two back-of-envelope analytic estimates, $1.0$ and $1.8$, bracket
the difficulty rather than resolve it.) Only the order $1-\beta_2\asymp1/\log\log X$,
and hence $\beta_2\to1$, is robust---which is all Theorem~\ref{thm:beta} asserts.

\begin{remark}[What is missing for a full proof]
Three gaps separate Theorem~\ref{thm:beta} from an unconditional statement: (i) the
uniform Hardy--Littlewood Assumption~\ref{ass:HL}, in particular uniformity in the
coefficient $r$ up to $\sqrt N$ (a Bombieri--Vinogradov-type input); (ii) a rigorous
bound on the correlation between the HL error terms and $\log\sing$, currently
absorbed into the $o(1)$; (iii) control of the linearisation remainder, which is
genuinely $O(\widetilde W_0^{-2})$ by the absolute convergence of $A,B$ but requires
the tail of $\widetilde W$. None of these is available unconditionally, consistent
with $\beta_2\to1$ being a statement strictly stronger than the Hardy--Littlewood
asymptotics it is built on.
\end{remark}

\subsection{A first-moment reduction: replacing pointwise HL by equidistribution}
\label{sec:firstmoment}

Assumption~\ref{ass:HL} demands a \emph{pointwise} asymptotic for $C_r'(N)$, which for
fixed $N$ is as hard as the binary problem. The difficulty disappears if we pass to
\emph{first moments} over the window, because summing over $N$ replaces the binary
problem by counting primes in arithmetic progressions, which is unconditional
(Siegel--Walfisz) for fixed modulus and is the content of Bombieri--Vinogradov
uniformly. Define the conditional response ratios
\[
  A_\ell:=\frac{\langle R_1\rangle_{\ell\mid N}}{\langle R_1\rangle_{\ell\nmid N}},
  \qquad
  B_\ell:=\frac{\langle R_2\rangle_{\ell\mid N}}{\langle R_2\rangle_{\ell\nmid N}},
\]
where $\langle\,\cdot\,\rangle_{\ell\mid N}$ is the mean over $N\in\mathcal N_X$ with
$\ell\mid N$, and the first-moment exponent
\begin{equation}
  \beta_2^{(1)}(X)-1:=\frac{\sum_\ell\log(B_\ell/A_\ell)\,g_\ell\,v_\ell}
                          {\sum_\ell g_\ell^2\,v_\ell},\qquad v_\ell=\tfrac1\ell(1-\tfrac1\ell),
  \label{eq:beta-fm}
\end{equation}
the decomposition of \eqref{eq:beta-def} by prime $\ell$ in the independence model of
Lemma~\ref{lem:moments}.

\begin{lemma}[First-moment responses]\label{lem:fm}
Let $F_\ell=\langle\#\{p:\,\ell\mid N-p,\ N-p\in\SSemi\}\rangle/\langle R_2\rangle$ be
the proportion of semiprime representations whose semiprime is divisible by $\ell$.
If primes are equidistributed in the reduced residue classes mod $\ell$ (Siegel--Walfisz
for fixed $\ell$; Bombieri--Vinogradov for $\ell\le X^{1/2-\varepsilon}$ on average),
then
\[
  A_\ell=\frac{\ell-1}{\ell-2},\qquad
  B_\ell=\frac{(\ell-1)(1-F_\ell)}{\ell-2+F_\ell}.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Summing over $N\in\mathcal N_X$ removes the binary constraint:
$\sum_{\ell\mid N}R_1=\#\{(p,p'):p+p'\in\mathcal N_X,\ \ell\mid p+p'\}$. As $p,p'$ are
units mod $\ell$, equidistribution gives a proportion $1/(\ell-1)$ of pairs with
$p'\equiv-p$, so $\sum_{\ell\mid N}R_1=\frac{1}{\ell-1}\sum_NR_1$; dividing by the
counts $|\mathcal N_X|/\ell$ and $|\mathcal N_X|(\ell-1)/\ell$ yields
$A_\ell=(\ell-1)/(\ell-2)$. For $R_2$, fix $(r,s)$ and count $p=N-rs$ in the window:
$\ell\mid N$ forces $p\equiv-rs$. If $\ell\nmid rs$ this is a single reduced class
(proportion $1/(\ell-1)$); if $\ell\mid rs$ it forces $p\equiv0$, i.e.\ $p=\ell$, so
those representations (a fraction $F_\ell$ of $R_2$) are excluded. Hence
$\sum_{\ell\mid N}R_2=\frac{1-F_\ell}{\ell-1}\sum_NR_2$, and the same normalisation gives
$B_\ell=\frac{(\ell-1)(1-F_\ell)}{\ell-2+F_\ell}$.
\end{proof}

Both formulas are confirmed to four places (Table~\ref{tab:fm}); note $A_\ell$ is the
exact singular factor, and $B_\ell<A_\ell$ by the blocked-channel deficit $F_\ell$.

\begin{theorem}[First-moment exponent, conditional on Bombieri--Vinogradov]\label{thm:fm}
Under the equidistribution input of Lemma~\ref{lem:fm} and with $F_\ell\sim$ the
channel weight $\frac{(\ell-1)/(\ell-2)\,w_\ell}{\widetilde W_0}$, the first-moment
exponent satisfies
\[
  \beta_2^{(1)}(X)=1-\frac{c^{(1)}}{\widetilde W_0(X)}+O(\widetilde W_0^{-2}),
  \qquad c^{(1)}=\frac{1}{B}\sum_{\ell>2}\Big(\tfrac{\ell-1}{\ell-2}\Big)^{2}w_\ell\,g_\ell\,v_\ell ,
\]
an absolutely convergent constant; hence $\beta_2^{(1)}(X)\to1$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
By Lemma~\ref{lem:fm}, $\log(B_\ell/A_\ell)=\log\frac{(\ell-2)(1-F_\ell)}{\ell-2+F_\ell}
=-\frac{\ell-1}{\ell-2}F_\ell+O(F_\ell^2)$. Inserting into \eqref{eq:beta-fm} with
$F_\ell=\frac{\ell-1}{\ell-2}w_\ell/\widetilde W_0+o(\cdot)$ gives the stated slope,
and $\widetilde W_0\sim\log\log X$ as in Theorem~\ref{thm:beta}.
\end{proof}

\paragraph{The reduced gap.}
Theorem~\ref{thm:fm} rests only on equidistribution of primes in progressions---a
theorem (Siegel--Walfisz; Bombieri--Vinogradov for uniformity in $\ell$)---together
with the first moment $F_\ell$, which is of Prime-Number-Theorem strength. The sole
remaining step to Theorem~\ref{thm:beta} is the passage from the first-moment exponent
to the pointwise (log-regression) one. Empirically this gap is a \emph{constant}
$\beta_2-\beta_2^{(1)}\approx0.004$, stable across $X=2\cdot10^{6}$--$10^{8}$
(measured $\beta_2^{(1)}=0.528,0.563,0.585$ against $\beta_2=0.531,0.567,0.589$): it is
the Jensen defect $\mathbb E[\log R_i]-\log\mathbb E[R_i]$, governed by the normalised
variance of $R_1,R_2$. That variance is exactly what the Montgomery--Vaughan
exceptional-set method controls for $R_1$ (the binary Goldbach asymptotic holds for all
but $O(X^{1-\delta})$ even $N$). An analogous second-moment bound for $R_2$ (which we
prove below, Proposition~\ref{prop:var}) yields the \emph{linear} almost-all asymptotic
$R_2=\mathrm{main}(1+o(1))$ off a sparse set. We are careful about the last step. A
variance ($L^2$) bound controls the \emph{quantity} $R_2-\mathrm{main}$, not its
\emph{logarithm}: the covariance \eqref{eq:beta-def} that defines $\beta_2$ involves
$\log(R_2/R_1)$, which is sensitive to the lower tail (where $R_2$ is small,
$\log(R_2/R_1)\to-\infty$), and an $L^2$ bound alone does not preclude a sparse set from
dominating that covariance. Passing from the linear statement to
``$\beta_2(X)\to1$ for almost all $N$'' therefore needs an additional input---a
lower-tail (log-integrability) control of $R_2/R_1$ off the exceptional set---which we
isolate explicitly in Corollary~\ref{cor:beta-aa} rather than absorb into an $o(1)$.
This already isolates the genuine remaining difficulty as a \emph{variance plus
lower-tail} estimate, not the pointwise asymptotic of Assumption~\ref{ass:HL}.

\subsection{The variance bound and the exceptional set}
\label{sec:variance}

We now show that the required variance is small, so the exceptional set is sparse and
the pointwise law holds for almost all $N$. The natural object is the concentration of
the \emph{ratio} on the channel prediction: set
\[
  b(N):=\frac{R_2(N)/R_1(N)}{\widetilde W(N)},
\]
so that $b(N)\equiv1$ would be the pointwise law $R_2/R_1=\widetilde W$, hence (taking
logs and regressing on $\log\sing$) $\beta_2(N)=\beta_2^{(1)}(X)$ for every $N$. The
measured fluctuation of $b$ is tiny and decreasing (Table~\ref{tab:conc}): the
coefficient of variation is below $0.7\%$ and \emph{no} $N$ in the window deviates from
the channel prediction by as much as $5\%$.

\begin{proposition}[Exceptional set, linear form]\label{prop:exc}
By Chebyshev, the density of $N\in\mathcal N_X$ with
$|R_2/R_1-\langle b\rangle\widetilde W|>\varepsilon\widetilde W$ is at most
$\mathrm{CV}(b)^2/\varepsilon^2$. With the measured $\mathrm{CV}(b)\le0.7\%$ this is
$\le0.2\%$ at $\varepsilon=0.05$ (and the empirical exceptional set is in fact empty).
Hence $R_2(N)/R_1(N)=\widetilde W(N)\,(1+o(1))$ for all but a density-$o(1)$ set of $N$.
This is the \emph{linear} almost-all statement; the further passage to
$\beta_2(N)\to1$ is Corollary~\ref{cor:beta-aa}.
\end{proposition}

\paragraph{The variance bound by the circle method.}
The concentration above is what a second-moment argument delivers, and---crucially---it
needs no sieve. Work on the dyadic window $N\in(X,2X]$ and set, with sums over
$n\le2X$,
\[
  S_\PP(\alpha)=\sum_{p}e(p\alpha),\qquad
  S_{\SSemi}(\alpha)=\sum_{q\in\SSemi}e(q\alpha),\qquad
  R_2(N)=\int_0^1 S_\PP(\alpha)S_{\SSemi}(\alpha)e(-N\alpha)\,d\alpha .
\]
Split $[0,1]$ into major arcs $\mathfrak M$ (denominators $q_0\le Q=(\log X)^{C}$) and
minor arcs $\mathfrak m$, and let $\mathrm{main}(N)=\int_{\mathfrak M}S_\PP S_{\SSemi}\,e(-N\alpha)$.

\begin{proposition}[Variance, unconditional]\label{prop:var}
\[
  \sum_{X<N\le2X}\big|R_2(N)-\mathrm{main}(N)\big|^2
  \ \le\ \int_{\mathfrak m}|S_\PP|^2|S_{\SSemi}|^2\,d\alpha
  \ \le\ \Big(\sup_{\alpha\in\mathfrak m}|S_\PP(\alpha)|\Big)^2\,\#\SSemi(2X).
\]
With $Q=(\log X)^{2A+8}$, Vaughan's minor-arc bound
$\sup_{\mathfrak m}|S_\PP|\ll X(\log X)^{-A}$ and the trivial Parseval identity
$\int_0^1|S_{\SSemi}|^2=\#\SSemi(2X)\ll X\log\log X/\log X$ give
\[
  \sum_{X<N\le2X}\big|R_2(N)-\mathrm{main}(N)\big|^2\ \ll\ X^{3}\,\log\log X\,(\log X)^{-2A-1}.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The first inequality is Bessel: $R_2(N)-\mathrm{main}(N)=\int_{\mathfrak m}S_\PP S_{\SSemi}e(-N\alpha)$
are Fourier coefficients of $S_\PP S_{\SSemi}\mathbf 1_{\mathfrak m}$, so their $\ell^2$
sum is at most $\int_{\mathfrak m}|S_\PP S_{\SSemi}|^2$. The second bounds one factor by
its sup on $\mathfrak m$ and integrates the other; $\int_0^1|S_{\SSemi}|^2=\#\SSemi(2X)$
since cross terms cancel. The displayed bound is Vaughan's estimate
($\sup_{\mathfrak m}|S_\PP|\ll(X Q^{-1/2}+X^{4/5})(\log X)^4$) at $Q=(\log X)^{2A+8}$.
\end{proof}

The only arithmetic input is the \emph{classical} Vinogradov--Vaughan bound for the
\emph{prime} sum; the semiprime sum enters through its $L^2$ norm alone---no bilinear
or sieve estimate for $S_{\SSemi}$ is required.

\begin{theorem}[Linear almost-all asymptotic for $R_2$]\label{thm:aa}
Since a typical $\mathrm{main}(N)\asymp X\log\log X/\log^2 X$, Proposition~\ref{prop:var}
gives, for $A\ge2$, a relative variance $\ll(\log X)^{3-2A}/\log\log X\to0$; by Chebyshev
the set of $N\in(X,2X]$ with $|R_2(N)-\mathrm{main}(N)|>\varepsilon\,\mathrm{main}(N)$ has
size $\ll_\varepsilon X(\log X)^{3-2A}=o(X)$. On the major arcs
$\mathrm{main}(N)=\widetilde W(N)\,[2C_2\sing(N)I_1(N)]\,(1+o(1))$ by the equidistribution
of primes and semiprimes in progressions of modulus $\le Q$ (Siegel--Walfisz and its
semiprime convolution) together with Lemma~\ref{lem:sing}; and $R_1(N)=2C_2\sing(N)I_1(N)(1+o(1))$
for all but $O(X^{1-\delta})$ even $N$ by Montgomery--Vaughan~\cite{montgomeryvaughan1975}.
Hence, for all but $o(X)$ even $N\in(X,2X]$,
\[
  R_2(N)=\mathrm{main}(N)(1+o(1)),\qquad
  \frac{R_2(N)}{R_1(N)}=\widetilde W(N)(1+o(1)).
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{corollary}[$\beta_2\to1$, conditional on lower-tail control]\label{cor:beta-aa}
Suppose in addition that $\log(R_2(N)/R_1(N))$ is uniformly integrable over
$\mathcal N_X$---equivalently, that the exceptional set $\mathcal E_X$ of
Theorem~\ref{thm:aa} contributes $o(1)$ to the covariance \eqref{eq:beta-def}, which holds
as soon as $R_2(N)\gg_A \mathrm{main}(N)(\log X)^{-A}$ for $N\in\mathcal E_X$ (a one-sided
lower bound, strictly weaker than the full asymptotic but \emph{not} implied by the $L^2$
bound of Proposition~\ref{prop:var}). Then the contribution of $\mathcal E_X$ to
\eqref{eq:beta-def} is negligible, the regression slope is computed off
$\mathcal E_X$ where $R_2/R_1=\widetilde W(1+o(1))$, and $\beta_2(X)\to1$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{remark}[The honest status of the limit]
Theorem~\ref{thm:aa} is the result we actually establish in outline; Corollary
\ref{cor:beta-aa} is what is usually quoted as ``$\beta_2\to1$ for almost all $N$,'' but
it carries a genuine extra hypothesis. The gap is precisely the lower tail of $R_2$:
$L^2$ concentration of $R_2-\mathrm{main}$ bounds how often $R_2$ is \emph{large}, but the
log-regression that defines $\beta_2$ also weights $N$ where $R_2$ is \emph{small}, and a
sparse set with $R_2$ very small could in principle skew the covariance. The required
one-sided bound is mild but does not come directly from Chen's theorem: Chen's method
lower-bounds the \emph{combined} count $R_{\le2}=R_1+R_2$ (every large even $N$ has a
prime-or-semiprime partner), not the semiprime-only $R_2$ in isolation. To extract
$R_2\gg\mathrm{main}(N)$ one would pair such a lower bound on $R_{\le2}$ with an
\emph{upper} bound on $R_1$, which holds for all but $O(X^{1-\delta})$ even $N$ by
Montgomery--Vaughan~\cite{montgomeryvaughan1975}; the combination is reasonable but we do
not prove it here. The hypothesis is amply confirmed empirically---the exceptional set is
\emph{empty} at the $5\%$ level up to $5\cdot10^{8}$ (Table~\ref{tab:conc}), so in the
computed range no $N$ has $R_2/R_1$ even near the tail---but we flag it rather than claim
it.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}
The \emph{linear} Theorem~\ref{thm:aa} is unconditional in outline: every
ingredient---Vaughan's minor-arc bound \citep{vaughan1997}, Siegel--Walfisz,
Montgomery--Vaughan, and the Euler-product identity Lemma~\ref{lem:sing}---is classical.
A fully written proof must still supply the routine major-arc computation for the
semiprime sum (semiprimes in progressions of modulus $\le(\log X)^{C}$, which follow from
the prime distribution by Selberg--Sath\'e convolution \citep{selberg1954,sathe1953};
\S\ref{sec:majorarc}) and track the dependence on $A$. The genuinely \emph{new} content
is the singular-series cancellation of Lemma~\ref{lem:sing} and the resulting first-moment
law; the analytic machinery that turns it into the \emph{linear} almost-all theorem is off
the shelf. This is in sharp contrast to the pointwise Assumption~\ref{ass:HL}, which lies
beyond current technology. The remaining step to the \emph{log-regression} statement
$\beta_2\to1$ is the lower-tail input of Corollary~\ref{cor:beta-aa}, which is empirically
trivial in range but not proved.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Truncated logarithms and the blocked-channel model}
\label{sec:truncated}

The second-moment estimate of \S\ref{sec:variance} controls $R_2-\mathrm{main}$ in $L^2$,
not $\log(R_2/R_1)$, and Corollary~\ref{cor:beta-aa} flagged the lower tail as the
obstruction to the untruncated slope. We add two short results that delimit the gap exactly:
the second moment \emph{does} control a \emph{truncated} logarithmic statistic
(Proposition~\ref{prop:trunc}), and an idealized blocked-channel model proves the
$1/\log\log X$ deficit (Proposition~\ref{prop:blocked}). Neither establishes the untruncated
$\beta_2\to1$; together they show that only the lower tail, on a sparse set, is left open.

\paragraph{A truncated-log bridge.}
Write $\rho(N)=R_2(N)/\mathrm{main}(N)$ and, on a dyadic window
$\mathcal W=\{N\in(X,2X]:2\mid N\}$, let
\[
  \eta_X:=\frac1{|\mathcal W|}\sum_{N\in\mathcal W}\big|\rho(N)-1\big|^2 ,
\]
the relative variance bounded in Proposition~\ref{prop:var}/Theorem~\ref{thm:aa}:
$\eta_X\ll(\log X)^{3-2A}/\log\log X\to0$ for $A\ge2$. Fix a truncation level
$\varepsilon_X=(\log X)^{-A'}$ ($A'>0$ fixed) and set
$L_X(N)=\log\max\{\rho(N),\varepsilon_X\}$, the response with its lower tail clipped.

\begin{proposition}[Truncated logarithmic stability]\label{prop:trunc}
If $\eta_X\log(1/\varepsilon_X)\to0$ (which holds for the above $\varepsilon_X$ whenever
$A\ge2$), then
\[
  \frac1{|\mathcal W|}\sum_{N\in\mathcal W}\big|L_X(N)\big|
  \ \ll\ \big(\eta_X\log(1/\varepsilon_X)\big)^{1/3}=o(1),
  \qquad
  \frac1{|\mathcal W|}\sum_{N\in\mathcal W}L_X(N)^2=o(1).
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let $\delta_X\in(0,\tfrac12)$. On the good set $G=\{|\rho-1|\le\delta_X\}$ we have
$\rho\ge1-\delta_X>\varepsilon_X$ for large $X$, so $L_X=\log\rho$ and $|L_X|\le2\delta_X$. By
Chebyshev the complement has density $\le\eta_X/\delta_X^2$; there, if $\rho<1$ then
$\max\{\rho,\varepsilon_X\}\ge\varepsilon_X$ gives $|L_X|\le\log(1/\varepsilon_X)$, while if
$\rho\ge1$ then $R_2(N)\le N$ and $\mathrm{main}(N)\gg X\log\log X/\log^2X$ on $\mathcal W$
give $\rho\ll\log^2X/\log\log X$, hence $|L_X|=\log\rho\le2\log\log X+O(1)\le
C\log(1/\varepsilon_X)$. Therefore
$|\mathcal W|^{-1}\sum_{\mathcal W}|L_X|\le2\delta_X+C\,\eta_X\delta_X^{-2}\log(1/\varepsilon_X)$,
and $\delta_X=(\eta_X\log(1/\varepsilon_X))^{1/3}$ yields the first bound. The second follows
from the pointwise $|L_X|\le C\log(1/\varepsilon_X)$:
$|\mathcal W|^{-1}\sum L_X^2\le C\log(1/\varepsilon_X)\,|\mathcal W|^{-1}\sum|L_X|\to0$ for
$A\ge2$.
\end{proof}

Since $\log(R_2/R_1)=\log\rho+\log(\mathrm{main}/R_1)$ and
$\mathrm{main}(N)/R_1(N)=\widetilde W(N)(1+o(1))$ off a set of size $O(X^{1-\delta})$ (the
major arcs of \S\ref{sec:majorarc} and Montgomery--Vaughan for $R_1$),
Proposition~\ref{prop:trunc} shows that the $\log\rho$ part contributes $o(1)$ to both the
variance and the covariance of the $\beta_2$-regression once truncated. The \emph{truncated}
log-regression slope is therefore governed entirely by the channel term $\log\widetilde W$,
treated next. This is the precise sense in which the second-moment machinery already
suffices: the only quantity it cannot reach is the untruncated lower tail, which is
empirically inactive ($\mathrm{CV}(b)<0.7\%$ up to $5\cdot10^{8}$, Table~\ref{tab:conc}), so
in the computed range the truncated and untruncated slopes coincide.

\paragraph{The blocked-channel model.}
We make the mechanism behind $\beta_2(X)=1-c/\widetilde W_0$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:beta})
precise in the Kubilius-type model of Lemma~\ref{lem:moments}, where the indicators
$e_\ell=\mathbf 1[\ell\mid N]$ are independent Bernoulli$(1/\ell)$ over distinct odd primes.
Freezing the weights at scale $X$, with $a_r=\frac{r-1}{r-2}\frac1r$ set
\[
  W_{0,X}=\!\!\sum_{3\le r\le\sqrt X}\!\!a_r,\qquad
  D_X(N)=\!\!\sum_{\substack{r\mid N\\3\le r\le\sqrt X}}\!\!a_r,\qquad
  W_X(N)=W_{0,X}-D_X(N),
\]
and, with $g_\ell=\log\frac{\ell-1}{\ell-2}$, $Y_X(N)=\sum_{\ell\mid N,\,\ell>2}g_\ell$ (the
model's $\log\sing$).

\begin{proposition}[Blocked-channel slope; model]\label{prop:blocked}
In the independent-divisibility model,
\[
  \operatorname{Cov}(\log W_X,\,Y_X)=-\frac{A_X}{W_{0,X}}+O\!\big(W_{0,X}^{-2}\big),
  \quad A_X=\!\!\sum_{3\le r\le\sqrt X}\!\!a_r\,g_r\,\tfrac1r\big(1-\tfrac1r\big)>0,
\]
and $\Var(Y_X)=B_X:=\sum_{2<\ell\le\sqrt X}g_\ell^2\,\tfrac1\ell(1-\tfrac1\ell)\to B$. Hence
the model slope satisfies
\[
  \beta_{2,X}^{\mathrm{model}}=1+\frac{\operatorname{Cov}(\log W_X,Y_X)}{\Var(Y_X)}
  =1-\frac{C_X}{W_{0,X}}+O\!\big(W_{0,X}^{-2}\big),\qquad C_X=\frac{A_X}{B_X},
\]
with $A_X,B_X$ positive and bounded; since $W_{0,X}=\log\log X+O(1)$ (Mertens),
\[
  \boxed{\,1-\beta_{2,X}^{\mathrm{model}}\ \asymp\ \frac1{\log\log X}.\,}
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
As $\mathbb E[D_X]=\sum_r a_r/r=O(1)$ while $W_{0,X}\to\infty$, the event
$\{D_X\le\tfrac12 W_{0,X}\}$ has complementary probability $O(W_{0,X}^{-2})$ (Chebyshev), and
there $\log W_X=\log W_{0,X}-D_X/W_{0,X}+O(D_X^2/W_{0,X}^2)$; hence
$\operatorname{Cov}(\log W_X,Y_X)=-W_{0,X}^{-1}\operatorname{Cov}(D_X,Y_X)+O(W_{0,X}^{-2})$. By
independence $\operatorname{Cov}(e_r,e_\ell)=\delta_{r\ell}\,\tfrac1r(1-\tfrac1r)$, so
$\operatorname{Cov}(D_X,Y_X)=\sum_r a_rg_r\tfrac1r(1-\tfrac1r)=A_X$ and
$\Var(Y_X)=B_X$; both series converge ($a_rg_r/r\asymp r^{-3}$, $g_\ell^2/\ell\asymp\ell^{-3}$),
and $W_{0,X}=\sum_{r\le\sqrt X}\frac{r-1}{(r-2)r}=\sum_{r\le\sqrt X}\tfrac1r+O(1)=\log\log X+O(1)$.
\end{proof}

This model theorem isolates the central mechanism: each unblocked semiprime channel inherits
$\sing(N)$ in full, but the same small-prime divisibility that \emph{raises} $\sing(N)$
(through $g_\ell>0$) \emph{removes} the matching $r$-channel from the Mertens sum (through
$-a_r$ in $D_X$). The covariance is therefore negative, purely mechanical, and
multi-channel---a diagonal sum over $r=\ell$ with no dominant term, consistent with the
$\sim10\%$ weight of the $3\mid N$ atom in \S\ref{sec:singexp}---and of size
$1/W_{0,X}\sim1/\log\log X$. It makes the constant $c=A/B$ of Theorem~\ref{thm:beta} precise
\emph{within the model}; it is not a theorem about the primes, for which the same identity
needs the unproven uniformity of Assumption~\ref{ass:HL}.

\begin{remark}[Chen-rescue threshold; heuristic]
Model the number of balanced Goldbach pairs in the band $p\in[N/2-k,\,N/2]$ as Poisson with
mean $\lambda_k(N)\approx 2C_2\,\sing(N)\,k/\log^2(N/2)$ (the Hardy--Littlewood density on a
band of $k$ integers). The chance of no prime pair is then $e^{-\lambda_k}$, so the fraction
of $N$ that require a semiprime rises as the band narrows and crosses one half near
$\lambda_k\approx\log2$, i.e.\ at
\[
  k_\star(N)\approx\frac{\log^2N}{2C_2\,\sing(N)}.
\]
This reproduces both features observed in \S\ref{sec:opt-emp}: the peak band-scale grows like
$\log^2N$, and a large $\sing(N)$ pushes the threshold to \emph{smaller} $k$ (primes are
locally abundant, so balance must be tighter before semiprimes are needed). The Poisson step
is heuristic---it ignores correlations among nearby pairs.
\end{remark}

\subsection{The \texorpdfstring{$\Omega$}{Omega}-generating function and singular-series response}
\label{sec:omega-gf}

The layer experiment of \S\ref{sec:layers-emp} can be organized by a single generating
function. For an even integer $N$, define
\[
  Z_N(z):=\sum_{p<N}\ones_{\PP}(p)\,z^{\OmegaF(N-p)} .
\]
Then $[z^k]Z_N(z)$ is the number of representations $N=p+q$ with $p$ prime and
$\OmegaF(q)=k$; in particular $[z]Z_N(z)=R_1(N)$ and $[z^2]Z_N(z)=R_2(N)$. For $z=e^{-\beta}$
this is the one-sided arithmetic partition function for the $\OmegaF$-energy of the second
summand (its two-sided analogue is the energy reframing of Appendix~\ref{app:reframings}).

The following is a \emph{model} statement, not a theorem about the primes: it is exact in the
local blocked-channel model of \S\ref{sec:truncated}, and it explains why a large singular
series pushes the second summand toward lower arithmetic complexity. Fix $y$ and let
$\mathcal P_y$ be the odd primes $\ell\le y$; model $B_\ell:=\ones_{\ell\mid N}$ as
independent Bernoulli$(1/\ell)$ (Lemma~\ref{lem:moments}). With $g_\ell=\log\frac{\ell-1}{\ell-2}$
as before, the model $\log\sing$ is $Y_y(N):=\sum_{\ell\in\mathcal P_y}g_\ell B_\ell$. Given
the divisibility vector $B=(B_\ell)$, the admissible second summand $q=N-p$ has local
divisibility probabilities
\[
  \Pr(\ell\mid q\mid B_\ell)=\pi_\ell(B_\ell):=
  \begin{cases}0,&B_\ell=1,\\[1mm]\dfrac1{\ell-1},&B_\ell=0,\end{cases}
\]
since $\ell\mid N$ blocks the channel ($q\equiv-p\not\equiv0$ for admissible
$p\not\equiv0\pmod\ell$), whereas if $\ell\nmid N$ exactly one admissible residue of $p$
gives $q\equiv0\pmod\ell$. Let
\[
  F_y(z;B):=\prod_{\ell\in\mathcal P_y}\big(1+(z-1)\pi_\ell(B_\ell)\big)
  =\prod_{\ell\in\mathcal P_y}\Big(1+(z-1)\tfrac{1-B_\ell}{\ell-1}\Big)
\]
be the local $\OmegaF$-generating function of $q$ after the inherited Goldbach singular
factor is divided out.

\begin{proposition}[Generating-function response; model]\label{prop:omega-gf}
Define the model singular response
$\beta_y(z)-1:=\operatorname{Cov}\!\big(\log F_y(z;B),\,Y_y(B)\big)/\Var(Y_y(B))$. Then, for
every fixed $z>0$ for which the logarithms are defined,
\[
  \beta_y(z)-1=-\,
  \frac{\sum_{\ell\in\mathcal P_y}\frac1\ell\big(1-\frac1\ell\big)\,g_\ell\,\log\!\big(1+\frac{z-1}{\ell-1}\big)}
       {\sum_{\ell\in\mathcal P_y}\frac1\ell\big(1-\frac1\ell\big)\,g_\ell^2}.
\]
Consequently $\beta_y(z)<1$ for $z>1$, $\beta_y(z)>1$ for $0<z<1$, and $\beta_y(1)=1$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Write $\log F_y(z;B)=\sum_{\ell}(1-B_\ell)\,h_\ell(z)$ with
$h_\ell(z):=\log\!\big(1+\frac{z-1}{\ell-1}\big)$, and $Y_y(B)=\sum_\ell g_\ell B_\ell$. By
independence the off-diagonal covariances vanish, so
\[
  \operatorname{Cov}(\log F_y,Y_y)
  =\sum_\ell h_\ell(z)\,g_\ell\,\operatorname{Cov}(1-B_\ell,B_\ell)
  =-\sum_\ell h_\ell(z)\,g_\ell\,\tfrac1\ell\big(1-\tfrac1\ell\big),
\]
and $\Var(Y_y)=\sum_\ell g_\ell^2\,\tfrac1\ell(1-\tfrac1\ell)$. Dividing gives the formula. As
$g_\ell>0$ for every odd prime, the sign is that of $h_\ell(z)$: positive for $z>1$, negative
for $0<z<1$, zero at $z=1$.
\end{proof}

The singular series therefore acts on the whole $\OmegaF$-generating function, not only on
the isolated layers $R_1,R_2$: weights $z>1$ overweight high complexity, where the response
falls below one (large $\sing$ suppresses the high-$\OmegaF$ tail), and $0<z<1$ overweight
low complexity, where it rises above one (large $\sing$ favours low-$\OmegaF$ summands). The
sign reversal of \S\ref{sec:layers-emp} is the coefficient-level shadow of this single
response.

\paragraph{Stochastic-dominance form.}
Let $\OmegaF_y(q):=\sum_{\ell\in\mathcal P_y}\ones_{\ell\mid q}$ in the same model;
conditional on $B$ its summands are independent Bernoulli$(\pi_\ell(B_\ell))$. If $B'\ge B$
coordinatewise then $\pi_\ell(B'_\ell)\le\pi_\ell(B_\ell)$ for every $\ell$, so
$\OmegaF_y(q)\mid B'\preceq_{\mathrm{st}}\OmegaF_y(q)\mid B$: adding blockers shifts the
factor-count law to the left. Since $Y_y(B)$ is increasing in $B$, for any increasing
$\varphi$ the map $B\mapsto\mathbb E[\varphi(\OmegaF_y(q))\mid B]$ is decreasing, and the
Harris--FKG inequality for product measures gives
$\operatorname{Cov}\big(\mathbb E[\varphi(\OmegaF_y(q))\mid B],\,Y_y(B)\big)\le0$. Taking
$\varphi=\ones_{\{m\ge k\}}$ shows the high-complexity tail is negatively correlated with the
singular series; $\varphi=\ones_{\{m\le k\}}$ gives the positive correlation for
low-complexity events.

\paragraph{Poissonized layers.}
With $\mu_y(B):=\sum_{\ell\in\mathcal P_y}\pi_\ell(B_\ell)=\sum_\ell\frac{1-B_\ell}{\ell-1}$,
\[
  \operatorname{Cov}(\mu_y,Y_y)=-\sum_{\ell\in\mathcal P_y}\frac{g_\ell}{\ell-1}\,\tfrac1\ell\big(1-\tfrac1\ell\big)<0 .
\]
Approximating $\OmegaF_y(q)\mid B$ by a Poisson variable of mean $\mu_y(B)$ and linearizing
\[
  \log\Pr(\OmegaF_y(q)=k\mid B)=\mathrm{const}+\Big(\frac{k}{\bar\mu_y}-1\Big)\big(\mu_y(B)-\bar\mu_y\big)+\cdots
\]
about $\bar\mu_y=\mathbb E\mu_y$ shows, since $\operatorname{Cov}(\mu_y,Y_y)<0$, a positive
singular-series response for layers below the typical factor count ($k<\bar\mu_y$) and a
negative one above it ($k>\bar\mu_y$). This is the mechanism behind the transition from
positive response in the Goldbach and Chen layers to negative response for higher
$\OmegaF$-layers; it gives the direction and the sign reversal, not the exact numerical
$\beta_k$.

\subsection{Weak uniformity of the location measures}
\label{sec:weak-cont}

The experiments of \S\ref{sec:cont-emp} suggest that the representation clouds become
continuous after normalization: the empirical laws of $q/N$ for both prime and semiprime $q$
approach the uniform law on $(0,1)$, up to small endpoint effects. This is what the weighted
Hardy--Littlewood heuristic predicts. For a bounded $C^1$ test function
$\varphi:[0,1]\to\mathbb R$ write, when $R_t(N)\ne0$,
\[
  \mu_N^{(1)}(\varphi):=\frac1{R_1(N)}\!\!\sum_{\substack{p<N\\ p,\,N-p\in\PP}}\!\!\varphi\Big(\tfrac{N-p}{N}\Big),
  \qquad
  \mu_N^{(2)}(\varphi):=\frac1{R_2(N)}\!\!\sum_{\substack{p<N\\ p\in\PP,\,N-p\in\SSemi}}\!\!\varphi\Big(\tfrac{N-p}{N}\Big),
\]
the averages of $\varphi$ against the type-$t$ location measures of \S\ref{sec:cont-emp}.

\begin{hypothesis}[Weighted prime and semiprime channel asymptotics]\label{hyp:weighted}
For every bounded $C^1$ test function $\varphi$ and almost all even $N$,
\[
  \sum_{\substack{p<N\\ p,\,N-p\in\PP}}\varphi\Big(\tfrac{N-p}{N}\Big)
  =(1+o(1))\,2C_2\,\sing(N)\,\frac{N}{\log^2N}\int_0^1\varphi(x)\,dx ,
\]
and, for each unblocked semiprime channel $q=rs$, uniformly in the range of $r$ used in the
paper,
\[
  \sum_{\substack{s\in\PP\\ N-rs\in\PP}}\varphi\Big(\tfrac{rs}{N}\Big)
  =(1+o(1))\,2C_2\,\sing(N)\,\frac{r-1}{r-2}\,\frac{N}{r\log^2N}\int_0^1\varphi(x)\,dx ,
\]
the channel omitted when $r\mid N$.
\end{hypothesis}

\begin{proposition}[Weak uniformity of the location measures; conditional]\label{prop:weakcont}
Under Hypothesis~\ref{hyp:weighted}, for almost all even $N$,
\[
  \mu_N^{(1)}(\varphi)=\int_0^1\varphi(x)\,dx+o(1),
  \qquad
  \mu_N^{(2)}(\varphi)=\int_0^1\varphi(x)\,dx+o(1) .
\]
Equivalently, the empirical measures of $q/N$ in the prime and semiprime channels converge
weakly to the uniform law on $(0,1)$, in the same smoothed/almost-all sense as the weighted
asymptotics.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For the prime channel, the first weighted asymptotic with $\varphi\equiv1$ gives
$R_1(N)=(1+o(1))\,2C_2\sing(N)N/\log^2N$; dividing the two estimates yields
$\mu_N^{(1)}(\varphi)=\int_0^1\varphi+o(1)$. For the semiprime channel, writing $q=rs$ and
summing the per-channel asymptotic over the unblocked $r\le\sqrt N$,
\[
  \sum_{\substack{p<N\\ p\in\PP,\,N-p\in\SSemi}}\varphi\Big(\tfrac{N-p}{N}\Big)
  =(1+o(1))\,2C_2\sing(N)\,\frac{N}{\log^2N}
  \Big(\!\!\sum_{\substack{3\le r\le\sqrt N\\ r\nmid N}}\!\!\frac{r-1}{r-2}\frac1r\Big)\int_0^1\varphi(x)\,dx ,
\]
the parenthesis being the Mertens channel sum $\widetilde W(N)$. Taking $\varphi\equiv1$ gives
the matching asymptotic for $R_2(N)$; dividing cancels both the inherited singular series
$\sing(N)$ and the channel sum, leaving $\mu_N^{(2)}(\varphi)=\int_0^1\varphi+o(1)$. This is
weak convergence against every bounded $C^1$ test function.
\end{proof}

This explains why the discontinuous pointwise representation sets nonetheless produce smooth
limiting clouds: the singular series governs the local \emph{density} of representations, but
after normalizing each measure it cancels---together with the Mertens channel sum in the
semiprime case---leaving the uniform spatial law. It is the conditional counterpart of the
weak-continuity diagnostics of \S\ref{sec:cont-emp}, and like the other appendix results it is
conditional on the stated weighted asymptotics, not an unconditional theorem about primes.

\subsection{The least-unblocked-prime law and residual collapse}
\label{sec:least-unblocked}

The residual exponent of \S\ref{sec:balance-emp} suggests that the Chen surplus is not merely
large: for most integers it collapses to the first locally admissible semiprime channel. This
can be formalized under the same kind of fixed-channel almost-all input that underlies the
circle-method discussion above.

For an even integer $N$, define the nondegenerate minimal multiplier
\[
  r_\star(N):=\min\{r\ge3:\ r\in\PP,\ \exists\,p,s\in\PP,\ N=p+rs\} .
\]
(In \S\ref{sec:balance-emp} the multiplier ranges over all primes; we restrict to $r\ge3$ to
exclude the parity-degenerate channel $r=2$, which forces $p=N-2s$ even, hence $p=2$ and
$s=N/2-1$ prime---a density-zero family. When $r\mid N$ the congruence $p=N-rs\equiv0\pmod r$
likewise forces $p=r$, a density-zero exception for each fixed $r$; so the two definitions
agree for almost all $N$.) Let
\[
  \rho(N):=\min\{r\ge3:\ r\in\PP,\ r\nmid N\}
\]
be the least locally unblocked odd prime. We use the following fixed-channel almost-all
hypothesis---an averaged, single-channel form of Assumption~\ref{ass:HL}.

\begin{hypothesis}[Fixed-channel almost-all asymptotic]\label{hyp:fixedchannel}
For every fixed finite set $\mathcal R$ of odd primes and every fixed $0<\eta<1/2$, for all
but $o_{\mathcal R,\eta}(X)$ even $N\le X$, uniformly for every $r\in\mathcal R$ with
$r\nmid N$,
\[
  C_{r,\eta}(N):=\#\Big\{s\in\PP:\ \eta\tfrac Nr\le s\le(1-\eta)\tfrac Nr,\ \ N-rs\in\PP\Big\}
  =(1+o(1))\,2C_2\,\sing(N)\,\frac{r-1}{r-2}\,\frac{(1-2\eta)N}{r\log^2N}.
\]
In particular, for every fixed unblocked $r$ the channel $N=p+rs$ contains a representation
with $s\asymp N/r$ for almost all even $N$.
\end{hypothesis}

\begin{theorem}[Least-unblocked-prime law; conditional]\label{thm:leastunblocked}
Assume Hypothesis~\ref{hyp:fixedchannel}. Then $r_\star(N)=\rho(N)$ for almost all even
integers $N$, and consequently $a_{\rm Res}(N)\to1$ for almost all even integers $N$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Fix a finite set of odd primes $\mathcal R_Y:=\{3\le r\le Y:\ r\in\PP\}$, and let $\rho_Y(N)$
be the least prime in $\mathcal R_Y$ not dividing $N$, when one exists. Consider an even
$N\le X$ for which $\rho_Y(N)$ exists. Every odd prime $r<\rho_Y(N)$ in $\mathcal R_Y$ divides
$N$, so its channel is locally blocked: $p=N-rs\equiv0\pmod r$, whence $p$ is prime only if
$p=r$, forcing $s=N/r-1$. For fixed $r$ the set of $N\le X$ with $N/r-1$ prime has size
$O(X/(r\log X))=o(X)$; the finite union over $r\in\mathcal R_Y$ is exceptional of size
$o_Y(X)$, and outside it no blocked $r<\rho_Y(N)$ yields a representation.

On the other hand $\rho_Y(N)\nmid N$, so by Hypothesis~\ref{hyp:fixedchannel} applied to
$\mathcal R_Y$, for all but $o_Y(X)$ even $N\le X$ the channel $r=\rho_Y(N)$ carries a
representation $N=p+\rho_Y(N)s$ with $s\asymp N/\rho_Y(N)$. Hence, outside a set of size
$o_Y(X)$, $r_\star(N)=\rho_Y(N)$ whenever $\rho_Y(N)$ exists.

It remains to remove the cutoff $Y$. The even $N$ for which $\rho_Y(N)$ does not exist are
exactly those divisible by every odd prime $r\le Y$, of density
$\prod_{3\le r\le Y}\tfrac1r$ among even integers. Therefore
\[
  \limsup_{X\to\infty}\frac{\#\{N\le X\ \mathrm{even}:\ r_\star(N)\ne\rho(N)\}}{X/2}
  \le\prod_{\substack{3\le r\le Y\\ r\in\PP}}\frac1r ,
\]
and the product tends to $0$ as $Y\to\infty$, giving $r_\star(N)=\rho(N)$ for almost all even
$N$. Finally, on the same density-one set the representation has $r=r_\star(N)\le Y$ (outside
a set of arbitrarily small upper density) and $s\asymp N/r$, so
\[
  a_{\rm Res}(N)=1+\frac{\log r_\star(N)}{\log s}\le1+\frac{\log Y}{\log N+O_Y(1)} ,
\]
which tends to $1$ as $N\to\infty$ for each fixed $Y$; since the exceptional density can be
made arbitrarily small, $a_{\rm Res}(N)\to1$ for almost all even $N$.
\end{proof}

This gives a precise meaning to the empirical ``residual collapse'' of
\S\ref{sec:balance-emp}: the small multiplier is not mysterious in the model---it is usually
just the first prime channel not locally blocked by divisibility of $N$. In the
independent-divisibility model of Lemma~\ref{lem:moments} it predicts
\[
  \Pr(\rho(N)=3)=\tfrac23,\quad
  \Pr(\rho(N)=5)=\tfrac13\!\cdot\!\tfrac45=\tfrac4{15},\quad
  \Pr(\rho(N)=7)=\tfrac13\!\cdot\!\tfrac15\!\cdot\!\tfrac67=\tfrac2{35},
\]
so $\Pr(\rho(N)\in\{3,5,7\})=\tfrac23+\tfrac4{15}+\tfrac2{35}\approx0.9905$, explaining why a
very small multiplier dictionary covers almost all integers in the experiments. It also
clarifies the link to the singular series: the same small-prime divisibility that
\emph{increases} $\sing(N)$ \emph{blocks} the corresponding small $r$-channels, so the least
available Chen multiplier is governed by local obstructions. The statement is conditional on
Hypothesis~\ref{hyp:fixedchannel}---a standard fixed-channel almost-all input---and is not an
unconditional theorem about primes; granting that input, the residual collapse and the
distribution of $r_\star$ follow from a clean local-obstruction argument.

\subsection{The major arcs: semiprimes in progressions}
\label{sec:majorarc}

Here we supply the one routine ingredient left open in Theorem~\ref{thm:aa}: the
evaluation of $\mathrm{main}(N)=\int_{\mathfrak M}S_\PP S_{\SSemi}e(-N\alpha)$, which rests
on the distribution of semiprimes in progressions. Write
$\pi_2(x;q,a)=\#\{n\le x:n\in\SSemi,\ n\equiv a\ (q)\}$ and
$\SSemi^*(x;q)=\#\{n\le x:n\in\SSemi,\ (n,q)=1\}$.

\begin{lemma}[Selberg--Sath\'e in progressions]\label{lem:s2ap}
Fix $A\ge1$. There is $c=c(A)>0$ such that, uniformly for $q\le(\log x)^A$ and
$(a,q)=1$,
\[
  \pi_2(x;q,a)=\frac{\SSemi^*(x;q)}{\phi(q)}+O_A\!\big(x\,e^{-c\sqrt{\log x}}\big).
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Write each $n\in\SSemi$ as $n=p_1p_2$ with $p_1\le p_2$. For $(a,q)=1$ a contribution
requires $(p_1p_2,q)=1$, so
\[
  \pi_2(x;q,a)=\sum_{\substack{p_1\le\sqrt x\\(p_1,q)=1}}
  \Big(\pi\big(\tfrac{x}{p_1};q,a\bar p_1\big)-\pi\big(p_1;q,a\bar p_1\big)\Big),
  \qquad \bar p_1=p_1^{-1}\!\!\pmod q .
\]
Since $p_1\le\sqrt x$ gives $\log(x/p_1)\ge\tfrac12\log x$, the Siegel--Walfisz theorem
applies uniformly for $q\le(\log x)^A$:
$\pi(y;q,c)=\mathrm{li}(y)/\phi(q)+O(y\,e^{-c_1\sqrt{\log y}})$ for $(c,q)=1$. Summing the
main terms gives $\frac1{\phi(q)}\sum_{p_1\le\sqrt x,(p_1,q)=1}[\mathrm{li}(x/p_1)-\mathrm{li}(p_1)]
=\frac1{\phi(q)}\SSemi^*(x;q)$, the last identity being the same decomposition of
$\SSemi^*$ without the progression. The error is
$\ll\sum_{p_1\le\sqrt x}\tfrac{x}{p_1}e^{-c_1\sqrt{\log(x/p_1)}}
\ll x\,e^{-c_2\sqrt{\log x}}\sum_{p_1\le\sqrt x}\tfrac1{p_1}\ll x\log\log x\,e^{-c_2\sqrt{\log x}}$,
which is $O_A(x e^{-c\sqrt{\log x}})$. The dependence on $A$ enters only through the
Siegel--Walfisz constant $c_1=c_1(A)$ (ineffective, via Siegel's theorem).
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Exact local structure]\label{rem:exact}
For $q=\ell$ prime the lemma is exact in form: the semiprimes with $\ell\mid n$ are
precisely $\{\ell m:m\in\PP\}$, so $\pi_2(x;\ell,0)=\pi(x/\ell)$, while the
$\ell-1$ reduced classes share $\SSemi^*(x;\ell)=\pi_2(x)-\pi(x/\ell)$ equally. This is
confirmed to the integer at $x=4\cdot10^{6}$: $\pi_2(x;3,0)=102{,}383=\pi(x/3)$ and each
reduced class holds $344{,}302=(\pi_2-\pi(x/3))/2$. The class-$0$ suppression
$\pi(x/\ell)/\pi_2(x)\sim 1/(\ell\log\log x)$ is exactly the $\log\log$ deficit that
produces the channel weight $\widetilde W$ and the blocking of Lemma~\ref{lem:sing}.
\end{remark}

\paragraph{The main term.}
On a major arc $\alpha=a/q_0+\beta$ ($(a,q_0)=1$, $|\beta|\le Q/X$) the prime sum is
classical, $S_\PP(\alpha)=\frac{\mu(q_0)}{\phi(q_0)}v_1(\beta)+O(X e^{-c\sqrt{\log X}})$
with $v_1(\beta)=\int_2^{2X}e(t\beta)\,dt/\log t$. For the semiprime sum, splitting into
residues and applying Lemma~\ref{lem:s2ap} (and Remark~\ref{rem:exact} for the
non-reduced classes) yields
\[
  S_{\SSemi}(\alpha)=G_2(a,q_0)\,v_2(\beta)+O(Xe^{-c\sqrt{\log X}}),\qquad
  G_2(a,q_0)=\sum_{b\,(q_0)}e_{q_0}(ab)\,\gamma_{q_0}(b),
\]
where $\gamma_{q_0}(b)$ is the local density of semiprimes in class $b$ and
$v_2(\beta)=\int e(t\beta)\,d\Pi_2(t)$, $\Pi_2$ the smooth semiprime count. For $q_0=\ell$
a short computation from Remark~\ref{rem:exact} gives the multiplier
$G_2(a,\ell)=\frac{1}{(\ell-1)\pi_2(x)}\big(\ell\,\pi(x/\ell)-\pi_2(x)\big)$, and pairing it
with the prime factor $\mu(\ell)/\phi(\ell)=-1/(\ell-1)$ reproduces, after summation over
$a$ and multiplication over $q_0$, the local factor $(\ell-1)/(\ell-2)$ at primes
$\ell\mid N$ removed and the suppression at $\ell=r\mid N$---i.e.\ the singular series of
Lemma~\ref{lem:sing}. Hence
\[
  \mathrm{main}(N)=\mathfrak S_2(N;Q)\,J(N)\big(1+o(1)\big),
  \quad \mathfrak S_2(N;Q)=2C_2\,\sing(N)\,\widetilde W(N)+O(Q^{-1}),
  \quad J(N)\asymp\frac{X\log\log X}{\log^2 X},
\]
the tail $\mathfrak S_2(N)-\mathfrak S_2(N;Q)\ll Q^{-1}$ being the standard
absolutely-convergent completion of the singular series.

\begin{remark}[Numerical check of the main term]
The channel asymptotic underlying both Assumption~\ref{ass:HL} and Lemma~\ref{lem:sing}
can be tested directly. Computing the channel counts $C_r'(N)$ for $r=3,5$ over a sample
of $N$ near $2\cdot10^{6}$ (with $r\nmid N$) and the archimedean integral $I_r(N)$ by
quadrature gives
\[
  \frac{C_r'(N)}{2C_2\,\sing(N)\,\tfrac{r-1}{r-2}\,I_r(N)}=0.996\pm0.004\quad(r=3),
  \qquad 0.995\pm0.008\quad(r=5),
\]
confirming both the constant $2C_2\tfrac{r-1}{r-2}$ of Lemma~\ref{lem:sing} and the
exact weight $I_r/I_1$ to better than $1\%$ per $N$. (The crude
$w_r=\tfrac1r\log N/\log(N/r)$ used for back-of-envelope estimates overstates
$I_r/I_1$ by a constant factor for small $r$; this is the source of the discrepancy
between the leading and measured values of $c$.)
\end{remark}

\paragraph{Tracking of $A$.}
The three parameters are tied through a single $Q=(\log X)^{B}$. Lemma~\ref{lem:s2ap} and
the prime major arcs hold for $q_0\le Q$ with any fixed $B$ (taking $A=B$); their errors
are $O(Xe^{-c(B)\sqrt{\log X}})$, super-polynomially small. The variance
(Proposition~\ref{prop:var}) needs $Q=(\log X)^{2A_0+8}$ to give relative variance
$\ll(\log X)^{3-2A_0}$. Choosing $B=2A_0+8$ makes all three compatible, and the
exceptional set in Theorem~\ref{thm:aa} is then
\[
  \#\{X<N\le 2X:\ |R_2(N)-\mathrm{main}(N)|>\varepsilon\,\mathrm{main}(N)\}
  \ \ll_{\varepsilon}\ X(\log X)^{3-2A_0}+Xe^{-c\sqrt{\log X}}
  \ \ll_{C}\ \frac{X}{(\log X)^{C}}
\]
for any fixed $C$ (take $A_0=\tfrac{C+3}2$). The major-arc error never binds; the rate is
governed solely by the classical minor-arc input. This completes, in outline, the
reduction of the \emph{linear} almost-all asymptotic $R_2=\mathrm{main}(1+o(1))$
(Theorem~\ref{thm:aa}) to standard prime-distribution estimates, the only genuinely new
step being the singular-series identity of Lemma~\ref{lem:sing}. The log-regression
statement $\beta_2(N)\to1$ then follows from the lower-tail hypothesis of
Corollary~\ref{cor:beta-aa}, the one ingredient we do not prove.


\section{The infinite formulation and column generation}
\label{app:infinite}

The finite selection MIPs of \S\ref{sec:finitemip} are truncations of an
infinite-dimensional feasibility problem. Let $\EE=\{N\in\NN:N>2,\ N\equiv0\,(2)\}$; for
every $N\in\EE$, $p\in\PP$ with $p<N$, and type $t\in\{1,2\}$ take a binary $z_{N,p,t}$. The
infinite Chen-cover is
\[
  \sum_{\substack{p\in\PP\\N-p\in\PP}} z_{N,p,1}
  + \sum_{\substack{p\in\PP\\N-p\in\SSemi}} z_{N,p,2}\ \ge\ 1
  \qquad(\forall N\in\EE),
\]
and its type-$1$ restriction is the infinite Goldbach-cover; the binary Goldbach conjecture
is exactly the feasibility of the latter. One can also write a formal infinite continuity
objective
\[
  \inf_{(p_N,q_N)}\sum_{N\in\EE} w_N\!\left(
    \alpha\,|\tau(q_N)-\tau(q_{N-2})|
    +\beta\Big|\tfrac{q_N}{N}-\tfrac{q_{N-2}}{N-2}\Big|\right),
  \qquad w_N=N^{-2},
\]
a mathematically controlled version of asking whether the representation can be made
continuous in the limit; finite-window truncations recover the minimum-variation MIP of
\S\ref{sec:finitemip}.

\paragraph{Column-generation reading.}
The variables $z_{N,p,t}$ are columns, each a certificate that $N$ is covered by a type-$t$
representation: Goldbach columns $(N,p,q)$ with $p,q\in\PP$, Chen columns with $q\in\Ptwo$,
coupled across consecutive $N$ by the smoothness penalties. The master problem selects one
column per even integer and the pricing problem seeks a representation of desirable reduced
cost. As throughout, the interesting content is selection under structure---whether a
low-variation path threads the representation cloud---not feasibility.

\section{Supplementary reframings}
\label{app:reframings}

The reformulations below recast the Goldbach--Chen frontier in the languages of statistical
physics, network science, markets, simplicial topology, control, information theory, and
discrete geometry. In each the governing quantity is the same singular series $\sing(N)$, so
they are best read as one fact---$\sing$ sets the local densities---seen in several mirrors,
not as independent results; we keep them for the individual vocabularies and the honest
negatives.

\subsection{Goldbach as arithmetic ground-state energy}
\label{sec:energy-emp}

A single reformulation subsumes the counts $R_1,R_2$, the layers $\OmegaF(q)=k$, and
the partition-function viewpoint. Assign to each decomposition $a+b=N$ ($a,b\ge2$) the
\emph{arithmetic energy} $H(a,b)=\OmegaF(a)+\OmegaF(b)$, and define the ground-state
energy and level spectrum
\[
  E(N)=\min_{a+b=N}H(a,b),\qquad
  n_E(N)=\#\{a+b=N:\ \OmegaF(a)+\OmegaF(b)=E\}.
\]
Then $E(N)=2$ \emph{iff} Goldbach holds for $N$; the ground-state degeneracy is
$n_2(N)=R_1(N)$, and the first excited level is $n_3(N)=2R_2(N)$ (prime$+$semiprime,
Chen). The spectrum (Fig.~\ref{fig:energy}, left) is a hump peaked at the typical energy
$\langle H\rangle\sim2\log\log N$, with the Goldbach ground state sitting in its
low-energy tail; the gap $\langle H\rangle-E(N)\sim2\log\log N-2$ diverges.

The thermodynamics is computable for all $N$ at once, since the partition function is a
self-convolution,
\[
  Z_N(\beta)=\sum_{a+b=N}e^{-\beta H(a,b)}=(f_\beta*f_\beta)(N),\qquad f_\beta(n)=e^{-\beta\OmegaF(n)},
\]
so one FFT per $\beta$ yields $Z_N$, the mean energy $U_N(\beta)=-\partial_\beta\log Z_N$,
and the specific heat $C_N(\beta)=\beta^2\Var_\beta(H)$. As $\beta$ increases past a
``melting'' point $\beta^\star(N)$, the system localises onto the Goldbach ground state:
$U_N$ drops from $\sim2\log\log N$ to $2$ and $C_N(\beta)$ peaks at $\beta^\star$
(Fig.~\ref{fig:energy}, centre; mean $\beta^\star\approx2.1$). Strikingly, this melting
temperature is almost a deterministic function of the singular series:
$\operatorname{corr}(\beta^\star,\log\sing)=-0.92$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:energy}, right). Where
$\sing(N)$ is large the ground state is highly degenerate ($R_1\propto\sing$) and
entropically favoured, so Goldbach order melts at a \emph{lower} $\beta$. Thus the same
singular series that governs $R_1$, $\beta_2$ and the balance exponent also governs the
thermodynamic stability of the Goldbach ground state---a unified statistical-mechanical
picture in which Goldbach is the statement ``every even $N$ has ground-state energy $2$''.

\subsection{Adversarial robustness of the Goldbach graph}
\label{sec:robust-emp}

View the representations as a bipartite graph: even integers $N$ on one side, primes
$p$ on the other, with an edge $N\!\sim\!p$ when $N-p$ is prime; the degree of $N$ is
$R_1(N)/2$. How robust is Goldbach to deletion of primes? On a window of $1.5\cdot10^{4}$
even integers near $10^{6}$ we compare three adversaries (Fig.~\ref{fig:robust}).

\emph{Random deletion} is almost powerless: deleting each prime independently with
probability $\phi$ leaves $N$ representable unless all its $\sim R_1(N)/2$ disjoint pairs
are hit, an event of probability $(1-(1-\phi)^2)^{R_1(N)/2}$. Goldbach survives in the
\emph{whole} window until $\phi\approx0.97$; one may delete $95\%$ of all primes at random
and still represent every $N$.

\emph{Targeted deletion} is efficient but local: since the pairs $\{p,N-p\}$ of a fixed
$N$ are disjoint, breaking $N$ is a vertex cover of a matching, costing exactly
$R_1(N)/2$ primes; the cheapest target is the lowest-degree $N$, giving
$\kappa=\min_N R_1(N)/2$. In the window this is $4.6\%$ of the primes---an order of
magnitude less than the random threshold, but aimed at a single $N$.

\emph{Modular deletion} is the striking case. Delete the primes in one reduced class
$a\bmod m$. The survivors lie in $(\mathbb Z/m)^\times\setminus\{a\}$, and an $N$ is
destroyed when its class is missing from the survivors' sumset. For $m=3$, deleting the
class $1$ (i.e.\ \emph{half} of all primes) leaves only the class $2$, whose sumset is
$\{2+2\}=\{1\}$: every $N\equiv0,2\pmod3$ loses all its representations, destroying $59\%$
of the window (Fig.~\ref{fig:robust}, right). The same $50\%$ of primes removed at random
destroys nothing. Thus the adversary manufactures a \emph{local obstruction}---the exact
mechanism the singular series rules out for the primes themselves. The effect is confined
to the smallest moduli: $m=4$ deleting class $1$ kills $N\equiv0\bmod4$ ($50\%$), while for
$m\ge5$ the surviving classes already sum onto everything and no $N$ is fully obstructed.

In short, the Goldbach graph is \emph{random-robust but structure-fragile}: invulnerable to
losing almost all primes by chance, yet shattered by removing one arithmetic progression.
This is a network-science counterpart of the local--global theme: Goldbach's redundancy is
enormous, but it is arithmetic, not statistical, redundancy.

\subsection{Goldbach as a market: liquidity and the fair-weather substitute}
\label{sec:market-emp}

Read each even $N$ as a market that must clear by hiring two prime inputs $p+q=N$; the
liquidity is the number of matches, $L_G(N)=R_1(N)$. Chen lets one input be a
\emph{minimally composite substitute} (a semiprime), giving $L_C(N)=R_1(N)+R_2(N)$ and a
substitute spread $S_C(N)=R_2/R_1\approx2.3$: in normal times the Chen market is three
times more liquid. We ask whether the substitute also provides \emph{resilience} under a
supply shock that removes each prime with probability $\phi$. A Goldbach match survives
with probability $(1-\phi)^2$ (two primes), but a Chen match only with $(1-\phi)^3$,
because the semiprime $q=rs$ needs its two factors $r,s$ to survive as well. Hence the
expected surviving liquidities are $L_G(\phi)=R_1(1-\phi)^2$ and
$L_C(\phi)=L_G(\phi)+R_2(1-\phi)^3$, and the substitute spread \emph{decays linearly},
\[
  S_C(\phi)=\frac{L_C(\phi)-L_G(\phi)}{L_G(\phi)}=S_C(0)\,(1-\phi)\ \longrightarrow\ 0 .
\]
Solving $L(\phi)=1$ for the illiquidity threshold, both markets collapse at essentially the
same point, $\phi_G\approx\phi_C\approx0.989$, and the resilience premium
$\phi_C-\phi_G\approx2\cdot10^{-4}$ is negligible (Fig.~\ref{fig:market}). The economic
reading is clean and a little counter-intuitive: the Chen channel is \emph{fair-weather
liquidity}---abundant when primes are plentiful, but worth ever less precisely as they
become scarce, because the composite good carries a higher assembly cost. The semiprime
substitute deepens the market in calm times; it does not hedge the crisis.

\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{19_mercado.png}
\caption{Goldbach as a market under a prime supply shock $\phi$. Left: expected surviving
liquidity of the Goldbach and Chen markets, both crossing the illiquidity threshold near
$\phi=0.99$. Right: the Chen substitute spread $S_C(\phi)=S_C(0)(1-\phi)$ vanishes
linearly---no resilience premium.}
\label{fig:market}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Persistent topology of additive decompositions (a negative note)}
\label{sec:tda2}

It is tempting to filter additive decompositions of $N$ by arithmetic complexity rather
than geometric distance: form the complex
$K_N^{(k)}=\{\{a,b\}:a+b=N,\ \OmegaF(a)+\OmegaF(b)\le k\}$ and let $k$ grow. But for the
\emph{binary} problem this is topologically empty: each integer $a$ lies in the single
pair $\{a,N-a\}$, so $K_N^{(k)}$ is a disjoint matching for every $k$. Its $0$-dimensional
persistence is therefore exactly the energy spectrum of \S\ref{sec:energy-emp}---each pair
is a component born at its energy $\OmegaF(a)+\OmegaF(b)$ and never dying---and all higher
homology vanishes. There is no topological structure to discover beyond the spectrum.

Genuine higher dimension appears only at the \emph{ternary} level. For odd $N$, form the
graph on primes $p<N$ with an edge $\{p,q\}$ whenever $N-p-q$ is prime, so that each edge
\emph{is} a ternary representation $(p,q,N-p-q)$. At $N=99{,}999$ this graph has $9{,}591$
vertices and $3.9\cdot10^{6}$ edges (density $0.084$, minimum degree $2$, so it is
connected---a graph form of Helfgott's ternary theorem). Yet it is strongly
\emph{anti}-clustered: it contains far fewer triangles than a random graph of equal
density, so ternary representations do not aggregate into higher-order simplices. The
honest conclusion is that the additive-complexity filtration adds nothing to the spectral
and graph-theoretic pictures already developed; we record it as a negative result.

\subsection{Goldbach as control: the value of arithmetic information}
\label{sec:control-emp}

Finally, view the even integers as a sequence of markets to be cleared by a controller: at
each $N$ the controller picks a prime $p$ (the action) and clears if $q=N-p$ is prime.
Goldbach is the statement that a successful policy exists; the computational question is
which \emph{simple} policy clears fastest. A naive policy ignores the arithmetic of $N$; a
\emph{residue-aware} policy with information budget $B$ uses the residues $N\bmod\ell$ for
primes $\ell\le B$ to prefer $p$ with $N-p$ coprime to those $\ell$---i.e.\ it avoids
spending tries on partners forced to share a small factor. Over a window near $10^{6}$
(Fig.~\ref{fig:control}), the single-shot clearing probability rises from $18\%$ (naive) to
$39\%$ at $B=13$, and the expected number of tries in a sequential search falls from $5.5$
to $2.5$---a $55\%$ reduction. The marginal value of each prime of information is largest at
$\ell=3$ (the factor $\tfrac{\ell-1}{\ell-2}=2$) and decreases thereafter, exactly tracking
the singular series. Unlike the other reframings in this section, this one yields a
concrete \emph{algorithm} rather than a restatement: the residue-aware ordering is a drop-in
search heuristic---enumerate candidate $p$ in increasing order of the number of small primes
$\ell\le B$ that $N-p$ is forced to share with $N$---whose benchmark above (expected tries
$5.5\to2.5$ at $B=13$, a $55\%$ reduction) is a measured speed-up of the per-$N$ Goldbach
search---the cost of finding one representation for a given $N$, distinct from the global
verification $R_1>0$ for all $N\le X$, which the FFT already settles---not an analogy. The same local arithmetic the singular series encodes is thus directly
actionable.

\caption{Value of arithmetic information for the Goldbach controller. As the information
budget $B$ grows, the single-shot clearing probability (blue) rises and the sequential
clearing cost (red) falls; the gain per prime tracks the singular-series factor
$(\ell-1)/(\ell-2)$.}
\label{fig:control}
\end{figure}

\subsection{The information content of the comet}
\label{sec:info-emp}

The spectral saturation invites an information-theoretic reading: how compressible is the
comet, and what is irreducible? Decomposing the variance of $\log R_1(N)$ into a smooth
trend, the local arithmetic (the conditional mean given $N\bmod30030$, which captures
$\sing$ non-linearly), and a residual, we find (Fig.~\ref{fig:info})
\[
  \underbrace{81.1\%}_{\text{trend }N/\log^2N}\ +\ \underbrace{18.8\%}_{\text{singular series }\sing(N)}\ +\ \underbrace{0.11\%}_{\text{irreducible}} .
\]
The comet is thus $99.9\%$ compressible \emph{by this particular code}---the conditional
mean given $N\bmod30030=2\cdot3\cdot5\cdot7\cdot11\cdot13$. We stress that this figure is
relative to the chosen conditioning set, not a theorem: the ``irreducible'' $0.11\%$ is
what survives after removing structure up to the prime $13$, and a richer conditioning
(more primes, or non-residue features) would shrink it further. With that caveat, the
residual is informative: its power spectrum is \emph{flat}---white noise, in contrast to
the sharply peaked spectrum of $R_1$ itself (\S\ref{sec:spectral-emp})---with lag-$1$
autocorrelation $-0.04$ and standard deviation $2.7\%$, a few times the Poisson floor
$\langle R_1\rangle^{-1/2}=0.9\%$. So \emph{up to modulus $30030$} the Goldbach comet is
almost entirely a singular-series codeword and the residue carries no further \emph{small-modulus}
arithmetic signal; we do not claim the absence of all further structure, only that none is
visible below this conditioning.

\caption{Information budget of the comet \emph{relative to conditioning on $N\bmod30030$}.
Left: the variance of $\log R_1$ splits into a smooth trend ($81\%$), the singular series
($18.8\%$), and a residual ($0.11\%$) that is irreducible \emph{by this code} (richer
conditioning would shrink it). Right: the residual's power spectrum is flat---white noise,

\subsection{Discrete geometry of the Chen surface}
\label{sec:geom-emp}

Each Chen representation $N=p+rs$ ($q=N-p$ semiprime, $r=\mathrm{spf}(q)\le s$) is a lattice
point on the surface $p+rs=N$. In normalised logarithmic coordinates
$u=\log r/\log N,\ v=\log s/\log N$ the points fill the triangle $\{u\le v,\ u+v\le1\}$,
with the edge $u=0$ (i.e.\ $r$ bounded) the Goldbach boundary and the diagonal $u=v$ the
balanced semiprimes. Accumulating $6.6\cdot10^{7}$ solutions over a window near $10^{6}$
(Fig.~\ref{fig:geom}, left) shows a cloud organised into discrete vertical \emph{rays}---one
for each small prime $r=3,5,7,\dots$, since $r$ is prime---whose brightness decreases with
$r$: the mass piles up against the Goldbach edge.

Quantitatively, the Li--Liu exponent of a solution, $a=1+\log r/\log s$, has the spectrum of
Fig.~\ref{fig:geom} (right): it peaks near $a\approx1.1$ and decays toward the balanced
value $2$, with mean $1.38$ and \emph{median $1.30$}. Half of all Chen solutions already
satisfy $a<1.3$, and essentially all sit far below the uniform threshold $a=1.9$ of
Li--Liu. So the Chen solutions of a typical $N$ concentrate near the Goldbach boundary: the
semiprime second summand is, far more often than not, an unbalanced product with a small
factor---the geometric form of the residual-exponent and factor-balance findings of
\S\ref{sec:balance-emp} and \S\ref{sec:diag-emp}.

\caption{Discrete geometry of $p+rs=N$. Left: density of Chen solutions in
$(u,v)=(\log r/\log N,\log s/\log N)$, with discrete rays at each prime $r$ and mass against
the Goldbach edge $u=0$. Right: the exponent spectrum $a=1+\log r/\log s$, median $1.30$,
far below the Li--Liu threshold $1.9$.}

\end{figure}
